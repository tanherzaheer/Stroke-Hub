Rehabilitation and Complications

Prevention and management of complications after stroke, and the principles of multidisciplinary rehabilitation — the phase of care where most recovery is won or lost.

Stroke unit care MDT rehabilitation Complication prevention

1. Stroke unit care — the foundation

Organised stroke-unit care reduces death and dependency compared with care on general wards — the single most robust intervention across all stroke types. It works through coordinated multidisciplinary care, early complication prevention, and structured rehabilitation. Admit stroke patients directly to a hyperacute/acute stroke unit.

2. Early complications and their prevention

2.1 Swallowing and aspiration

  • Perform a swallow screen before giving anything orally (including oral medication). Keep nil by mouth until screened.
  • If dysphagia is present, involve speech and language therapy (SALT); consider modified consistencies or alternative feeding (NG tube) to maintain nutrition and hydration and reduce aspiration pneumonia.

2.2 Venous thromboembolism (VTE)

  • Use intermittent pneumatic compression (IPC) for immobile patients — supported by the CLOTS-3 trial (reduces DVT, some survival benefit). Apply early and continue while immobile.
  • Anti-embolism (graduated compression) stockings are NOT effective after stroke (CLOTS-1/2) and should not be used for this purpose.
  • Pharmacological prophylaxis (LMWH) is used selectively, weighing VTE risk against bleeding — and is avoided/deferred in haemorrhagic stroke until safe.

2.3 Other early issues

  • Pressure ulcers — repositioning, pressure-relieving surfaces, skin care.
  • Infection — pneumonia and urinary tract infection (avoid unnecessary catheters).
  • Falls — assessment and prevention.
  • Malignant oedema / raised ICP — monitor large infarcts for deterioration (see hemicraniectomy on the Ischaemic Stroke page).
  • Haemorrhagic transformation, seizures (treat if they occur; no routine prophylaxis), hyperglycaemia, fever, and post-stroke delirium — all managed proactively.
  • Continence — assess bladder and bowel; avoid indwelling catheters where possible.

3. Early mobilisation — but not too aggressive

Early mobilisation and avoiding prolonged bed rest are beneficial, but the AVERT trial showed that very early, high-intensity, high-dose mobilisation within the first 24 hours can worsen outcomes. The practical message: mobilise early and frequently, but with shorter, gentler sessions in the first day rather than aggressive out-of-bed activity.

4. The rehabilitation multidisciplinary team

Rehabilitation is goal-oriented, patient-centred, and delivered by an MDT:

  • Physiotherapy — mobility, balance, strength, gait.
  • Occupational therapy — activities of daily living, upper-limb function, cognition, equipment, home assessment.
  • Speech and language therapy — swallowing and communication (aphasia, dysarthria, apraxia of speech).
  • Dietitians, clinical psychology/neuropsychology, social work, rehabilitation medicine, and specialist nursing.

Early Supported Discharge (ESD)

For selected patients, an MDT delivers rehabilitation at home at the same intensity as inpatient care, shortening hospital stay without worse outcomes.

5. Longer-term impairments and their management

  • Spasticity — positioning, stretching, physiotherapy; botulinum toxin for focal spasticity; oral agents (e.g. baclofen) for generalised spasticity, balancing weakness/sedation.
  • Shoulder pain / subluxation — careful handling, positioning, and support of the hemiplegic arm.
  • Central post-stroke pain — neuropathic pain from (often thalamic) strokes; managed with neuropathic agents.
  • Post-stroke depression and anxiety — common and under-recognised; screen for mood and treat; emotionalism (pathological crying/laughing) may respond to antidepressants.
  • Fatigue — very common, often persistent; pace activity and address contributors.
  • Cognitive and communication impairment — aphasia, neglect, memory, and executive dysfunction; SALT and neuropsychology input.
  • Vision (hemianopia, neglect) and contractures — targeted therapy.

6. Reintegration and secondary prevention

Continue secondary prevention (see the TIA and Ischaemic Stroke pages): antithrombotics, statins, BP control, AF anticoagulation, and lifestyle measures.

  • Driving — patients must follow DVLA rules and not drive for the specified period; advise them to check current guidance.
  • Return to work, mood, sexual function, and carer support — address holistically; signpost stroke-support organisations.
