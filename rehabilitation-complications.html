Rehabilitation and Complications

Recovery is where most stroke care actually happens: structured multidisciplinary rehabilitation, and anticipating the complications that derail it.

MDT rehabilitation Dysphagia & VTE Mood & cognition

1. Overview

Organised stroke-unit care with early multidisciplinary rehabilitation — physiotherapy, occupational therapy, speech and language therapy, dietetics, nursing, psychology — is one of the most effective interventions in the whole pathway. Mobilisation should start early but not hyper-acutely: very early, high-dose mobilisation within 24 hours worsened outcomes in AVERT. Every patient needs a swallow screen before oral intake; dysphagia drives aspiration pneumonia, the commonest early medical complication.

Complication surveillance runs alongside rehabilitation: venous thromboembolism prevention with intermittent pneumatic compression (CLOTS 3) rather than routine stockings; spasticity managed with physiotherapy, splinting, and botulinum toxin; hemiplegic shoulder pain; continence problems; and the under-recognised burden of post-stroke depression, anxiety, fatigue, and cognitive impairment. Routine fluoxetine does not improve motor recovery and increases fracture risk (FOCUS). Rehabilitation ends with reintegration — driving regulations, work, and long-term secondary prevention.

2. Key points

Essentials

  • Stroke-unit multidisciplinary care improves survival and independence — it is the platform for everything else.
  • Mobilise early but not hyper-acutely: very early high-dose mobilisation worsened outcomes (AVERT).
  • Screen swallowing before any oral intake; dysphagia causes aspiration pneumonia, the commonest early complication.
  • VTE prevention: intermittent pneumatic compression (CLOTS 3), not routine compression stockings.
  • Actively look for post-stroke depression, fatigue, and cognitive impairment; fluoxetine is not routine (FOCUS).
  • Cover driving restrictions, return to work, and long-term secondary prevention before discharge.
