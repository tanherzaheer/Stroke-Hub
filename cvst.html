Cerebral Venous Sinus Thrombosis

Thrombosis of the dural sinuses or cortical veins — a stroke syndrome of the young that is treated with anticoagulation, even when it has bled.

Young patients Anticoagulation-led Venography

1. Overview

Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis (CVST) obstructs venous drainage, causing raised intracranial pressure and venous infarction — often haemorrhagic, and characteristically not respecting arterial territories. It predominantly affects younger patients, with a female predominance driven by oestrogen-containing contraception, pregnancy and the puerperium; other risk factors include thrombophilia, malignancy, infection (mastoiditis, sinusitis), dehydration, and head injury. Presentations range from progressive headache (the commonest symptom) to seizures, focal deficits, papilloedema from intracranial hypertension, and encephalopathy — cavernous sinus and deep venous system syndromes are distinct subtypes.

Diagnosis is by CT or MR venography; a plain CT may be normal or show only indirect signs. Treatment is anticoagulation-led — therapeutic low-molecular-weight heparin acutely, followed by oral anticoagulation for 3–12 months or longer depending on cause — and, counterintuitively, anticoagulation is indicated even in the presence of haemorrhagic venous infarction. Decompressive surgery is reserved for malignant mass effect, and endovascular therapy for selected deteriorating patients.

2. Key points

Essentials

  • Suspect CVST in a young patient with progressive headache plus seizures, focal signs, or papilloedema — especially with hormonal or prothrombotic risk factors.
  • Haemorrhagic infarcts that cross arterial territories, or bilateral parasagittal/thalamic changes, should trigger venography.
  • Diagnosis: CT venography or MR venography; plain CT alone cannot exclude it.
  • Anticoagulate with therapeutic LMWH even if there is haemorrhagic venous infarction, then oral anticoagulation for 3–12+ months.
  • Escalate for decompressive surgery in malignant raised intracranial pressure.
