Posterior Circulation Stroke

Vertebrobasilar territory strokes account for roughly one in five ischaemic strokes — and are the ones most often missed at first contact.

Vertebrobasilar Commonly missed Basilar occlusion

1. Overview

The posterior circulation — vertebral, basilar, and posterior cerebral arteries — supplies the brainstem, cerebellum, thalami, and occipital lobes. Presentations include vertigo, ataxia, diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia, visual field loss, reduced consciousness, and crossed signs (ipsilateral cranial nerve palsy with contralateral limb deficit). Because symptoms overlap with benign vestibular disease, posterior circulation strokes are disproportionately missed; in acute vestibular syndrome a properly performed HINTS examination (head impulse, nystagmus, test of skew) outperforms early imaging in skilled hands.

Imaging has real pitfalls here: non-contrast CT is insensitive in the posterior fossa, and even early MRI diffusion-weighted imaging can be falsely negative in the first 24–48 hours. Basilar artery occlusion is a neurological emergency with a poor natural history — thrombectomy is considered up to 24 hours (ATTENTION, BAOCHE). In younger patients, vertebral artery dissection is a leading cause and warrants dedicated vessel imaging (see the young/cryptogenic algorithm).

2. Key points

Essentials

  • Think posterior circulation with vertigo plus any other neurology, new ataxia, diplopia, dysarthria, or visual field loss.
  • HINTS examination beats early CT for separating vestibular neuritis from stroke in acute vestibular syndrome.
  • CT is insensitive in the posterior fossa; early MRI-DWI can be falsely negative — a normal scan does not exclude the diagnosis.
  • Basilar occlusion: consider thrombectomy up to 24 hours (ATTENTION / BAOCHE); escalate immediately.
  • Vertebral artery dissection is a leading cause in the young — image the vertebrobasilar system properly.
