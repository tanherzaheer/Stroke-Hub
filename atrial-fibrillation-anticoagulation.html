Atrial Fibrillation and Anticoagulation

Stroke prevention in atrial fibrillation, the timing of anticoagulation after an acute stroke, detecting AF after cryptogenic stroke, and alternatives when anticoagulation isn’t possible.

13–26% of ischaemic strokes DOACs first line Start earlier than old dogma

1. Why AF matters in stroke

AF is one of the most important treatable causes of ischaemic stroke, contributing to roughly 13–26% of ischaemic strokes. AF-related strokes tend to be larger and more disabling, and effective anticoagulation dramatically reduces recurrence — so identifying AF and managing anticoagulation well is central to stroke care.

2. Assessing stroke and bleeding risk

  • CHA₂DS₂-VASc estimates stroke risk in AF (points for Congestive heart failure, Hypertension, Age ≥75 [2], Diabetes, prior Stroke/TIA [2], Vascular disease, Age 65–74, Sex category). Anticoagulation is generally recommended once risk exceeds the low-risk threshold; a prior stroke or TIA already scores 2 and mandates consideration of anticoagulation.
  • HAS-BLED estimates bleeding risk. It is used to identify and modify reversible bleeding risk factors (uncontrolled BP, concomitant antiplatelets/NSAIDs, alcohol) — not to withhold anticoagulation from patients who need it.

3. Choice of anticoagulant

  • DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban, dabigatran) are first line for non-valvular AF — comparable or better efficacy than warfarin with less intracranial haemorrhage, no routine monitoring, and fewer interactions.
  • Warfarin (VKA) remains required for mechanical heart valves and moderate-to-severe mitral stenosis, where DOACs are contraindicated.
  • Dose adjustment is needed for renal function, age, weight, and interactions — check the specific product criteria and the BNF.
  • Do not use DAPT/antiplatelets as a substitute for anticoagulation in AF — they are far less effective and not a safer trade-off in most cases.

4. Timing of anticoagulation after an acute ischaemic stroke

This is a common, high-stakes decision — balancing early recurrence against haemorrhagic transformation. Practice has shifted toward earlier initiation.

  • The old “1–3–6–12 day” rule of thumb (delaying longer for larger/more severe strokes) was based on expert consensus, not trials.
  • ELAN (NEJM 2023): early initiation (guided by infarct size — earlier for small/moderate, a little later for large) was at least as safe and effective as later initiation, with a trend favouring early.
  • OPTIMAS (Lancet 2024): in ~3,600 patients (median NIHSS 5), starting a DOAC within 4 days was non-inferior to delaying 7–14 days, with no increase in intracranial haemorrhage — challenging the routine “wait 2 weeks” practice.
  • CATALYST (individual-patient meta-analysis of TIMING, ELAN, OPTIMAS, and START, Lancet 2025): supports early DOAC initiation after AF-associated ischaemic stroke.

Practical synthesis

For most patients, a DOAC can be started early (often within a few days), guided by infarct size, severity, and any haemorrhagic transformation. Very large infarcts or haemorrhagic transformation still warrant more caution and individualised timing. Heparin “bridging” is not needed — start the DOAC directly. Always confirm haemorrhage is excluded/stable on imaging first, and follow local protocol.

5. Detecting AF after stroke

AF is often paroxysmal and missed on a single ECG. In cryptogenic stroke, prolonged cardiac monitoring substantially increases detection:

  • Extended Holter, event recorders, and implantable loop recorders (CRYSTAL-AF, EMBRACE) find AF that standard monitoring misses.

ESUS — anticoagulate when AF is documented

Trials of empirical anticoagulation for embolic stroke of undetermined source (ESUS) without documented AF — NAVIGATE-ESUS (rivaroxaban) and RE-SPECT ESUS (dabigatran) — were negative. Anticoagulate when AF is documented, rather than empirically in all ESUS.

6. When anticoagulation isn’t possible — LAAO

For patients with AF and a genuine long-term contraindication to anticoagulation (e.g. recurrent serious bleeding), left atrial appendage occlusion (LAAO) is an option (PROTECT-AF, PREVAIL, PRAGUE-17; newer trials such as OPTION compared LAAO with DOACs). It is a specialist, MDT-guided decision.

8. Key teaching points

Remember

  • Prior stroke/TIA = CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥2 → anticoagulate unless a genuine contraindication.
  • DOACs are first line except mechanical valves and moderate-to-severe mitral stenosis (warfarin).
  • Start anticoagulation earlier than the old 2-week dogma for most AF-related strokes — the evidence (OPTIMAS, ELAN, CATALYST) supports early DOAC initiation without excess ICH; still individualise for large or haemorrhagic-transformed infarcts.
  • Look hard for paroxysmal AF in cryptogenic stroke with prolonged monitoring, but don’t anticoagulate ESUS empirically without documented AF.
  • HAS-BLED guides risk-factor modification, not withholding treatment.
