Atrial Fibrillation and Anticoagulation

Finding the fibrillation, weighing stroke risk against bleeding risk, choosing the anticoagulant, and deciding when to start it after a stroke.

Cardioembolism DOACs first line Timing after stroke

1. Overview

Atrial fibrillation (AF) raises ischaemic stroke risk roughly five-fold, and AF-related strokes are larger and more disabling than other subtypes. After any ischaemic stroke or TIA, detection matters: a 12-lead ECG is only the start, and prolonged ambulatory monitoring — up to implantable loop recorders in selected cryptogenic patients (CRYSTAL-AF) — substantially increases the yield of paroxysmal AF. Stroke risk is stratified with CHA₂DS₂-VASc and bleeding risk with ORBIT or HAS-BLED — bleeding scores flag modifiable factors rather than vetoing anticoagulation, and a prior stroke already places the patient firmly in the treat category.

DOACs are first line for non-valvular AF (RE-LY, ROCKET-AF, ARISTOTLE, ENGAGE AF-TIMI 48): at least as effective as warfarin with roughly half the intracranial haemorrhage. Warfarin remains standard for mechanical valves and moderate–severe mitral stenosis. After an acute ischaemic stroke, modern trial evidence (ELAN; OPTIMAS) supports earlier DOAC initiation than the traditional 1–3–6–12 day rule for many patients, individualised to infarct size and haemorrhagic transformation. Left atrial appendage occlusion is an option when long-term anticoagulation is genuinely contraindicated.

2. Key points

Essentials

  • Hunt for paroxysmal AF after stroke/TIA: prolonged monitoring, escalating to implantable loop recorders in cryptogenic stroke (CRYSTAL-AF).
  • CHA₂DS₂-VASc for stroke risk; ORBIT/HAS-BLED to identify modifiable bleeding factors — prior stroke/TIA means anticoagulation is indicated in almost all.
  • DOACs first line in non-valvular AF; warfarin for mechanical valves and significant mitral stenosis.
  • Timing after ischaemic stroke: earlier DOAC initiation is supported by ELAN and OPTIMAS, individualised to infarct size.
  • Antiplatelets are not an adequate substitute for anticoagulation in AF.
