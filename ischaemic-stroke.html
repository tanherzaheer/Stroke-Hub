Ischaemic Stroke

Occlusion of a cerebral artery leading to focal brain infarction — the most common stroke subtype, and a time-critical medical emergency.

~85% of strokes Time-critical Reperfusion therapy

1. Definition & classification

Ischaemic stroke is a sudden focal neurological deficit caused by inadequate blood supply to a region of the brain, resulting in tissue infarction. It accounts for roughly 85% of all strokes, with the remainder being haemorrhagic (see Haemorrhagic Stroke).

Ischaemic stroke is commonly classified using the TOAST criteria by presumed mechanism:

  • Large-artery atherosclerosis — atherothrombosis or artery-to-artery embolism, e.g. carotid or intracranial stenosis.
  • Cardioembolism — most commonly atrial fibrillation; also valvular disease, recent myocardial infarction, and left ventricular thrombus.
  • Small-vessel (lacunar) disease — occlusion of a single perforating arteriole, typically related to chronic hypertension and diabetes.
  • Stroke of other determined aetiology — e.g. dissection, vasculitis, hypercoagulable states, venous sinus thrombosis.
  • Stroke of undetermined aetiology (cryptogenic) — no cause found despite investigation, or more than one plausible cause identified.

Clinically, the Oxford (Bamford) classification is widely used at the bedside to describe the syndrome based on examination findings alone, before imaging: Total Anterior Circulation Stroke (TACS), Partial Anterior Circulation Stroke (PACS), Lacunar Stroke (LACS), and Posterior Circulation Stroke (POCS).

2. Pathophysiology

Ischaemic stroke results from a critical reduction in cerebral blood flow (CBF) below the threshold required to sustain neuronal function and, ultimately, structural integrity.

  • Ischaemic core: tissue with CBF low enough (typically <10 mL/100 g/min) to cause rapid, irreversible cell death.
  • Ischaemic penumbra: surrounding tissue that is functionally impaired but structurally viable (CBF roughly 10–20 mL/100 g/min), kept alive by collateral circulation. This is the target of reperfusion therapy — "time is brain," with an estimated 1.9 million neurons lost per minute of large-vessel occlusion left untreated.

At the cellular level, energy failure leads to loss of ionic gradients, glutamate-mediated excitotoxicity, calcium influx, free radical generation, and eventually necrosis and apoptosis. Without reperfusion, the penumbra is progressively recruited into the infarct core over hours.

Key concept

The entire rationale for hyperacute stroke treatment (thrombolysis and thrombectomy) is to salvage the penumbra before it becomes irreversibly infarcted — hence strict time windows and, increasingly, imaging-based selection to identify patients with salvageable tissue beyond fixed time cut-offs.

4. Clinical presentation

Presentation depends on the vascular territory affected, but the hallmark is a sudden-onset, focal neurological deficit. Public and prehospital recognition tools include:

  • FAST — Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call emergency services.
  • BE-FAST — adds Balance and Eyes (visual disturbance) to improve sensitivity for posterior circulation strokes.
  • ROSIER scale — used in emergency departments to differentiate stroke from mimics (score >0 suggests stroke is likely).

Common syndromes by territory

Standardised severity assessment

The NIH Stroke Scale (NIHSS) is the standard tool for quantifying deficit severity (0–42), guiding treatment decisions, and tracking change over time.

5. Investigations

Immediate (hyperacute)

  • Non-contrast CT head — first-line to exclude haemorrhage; early ischaemic changes may be subtle (loss of grey-white differentiation, sulcal effacement, hyperdense artery sign).
  • CT angiography (CTA) head and neck — identifies large-vessel occlusion (LVO) amenable to thrombectomy.
  • CT perfusion (CTP) or MR diffusion/perfusion imaging — estimates core-vs-penumbra mismatch, particularly useful for extended-window thrombectomy decisions (see DAWN and DEFUSE-3 below).
  • Capillary glucose (hypoglycaemia is an important stroke mimic), ECG, and baseline bloods (full blood count, coagulation, renal function).

Subsequent work-up (aetiology)

  • Carotid Doppler ultrasound or CTA/MRA to assess for extracranial carotid stenosis.
  • 12-lead ECG and prolonged cardiac (Holter) monitoring to detect paroxysmal atrial fibrillation.
  • Echocardiography (transthoracic ± transoesophageal) if a cardioembolic source is suspected, particularly in cryptogenic stroke or younger patients.
  • Fasting lipid profile and HbA1c for vascular risk factor assessment.
  • MRI brain with diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI) — more sensitive than CT for confirming and localising acute infarction.
  • Thrombophilia and vasculitis screen, or vessel-wall imaging, selectively in younger patients or where dissection/vasculitis is suspected.

6. Management

6.1 Hyperacute reperfusion therapy

Intravenous thrombolysis with alteplase (or, increasingly, tenecteplase) is indicated within 4.5 hours of clearly defined symptom onset (or last known well) in eligible patients without contraindications such as recent surgery, active bleeding, anticoagulation above threshold, or haemorrhage on CT. Some services now use perfusion-imaging selection to extend treatment to wake-up strokes or unknown-onset strokes.

Mechanical thrombectomy is indicated for confirmed large-vessel occlusion (typically internal carotid or proximal MCA, M1/M2) and can be performed up to 24 hours from last known well in carefully selected patients using perfusion/collateral imaging criteria (see DAWN and DEFUSE-3). Thrombectomy is complementary to, not a replacement for, thrombolysis where both are indicated ("bridging therapy").

Red flags / contraindications to thrombolysis

Absolute contraindications include intracranial haemorrhage on imaging, active internal bleeding, recent major surgery or head trauma, and severe uncontrolled hypertension. Always follow your local thrombolysis protocol and checklist — see Local Guidelines.

6.2 Acute supportive care

  • Admission to a specialist stroke unit — consistently shown to reduce death and disability regardless of stroke severity.
  • Maintain normoglycaemia, normothermia, and adequate oxygenation; avoid aggressive blood pressure lowering in the acute non-thrombolysed patient unless extreme (permissive hypertension supports penumbral perfusion) — different thresholds apply if thrombolysis is given.
  • Early swallow screening before oral intake to reduce aspiration risk.
  • Antiplatelet therapy (typically aspirin 300 mg) started at 24 hours post-thrombolysis, or immediately if thrombolysis/thrombectomy is not given, once haemorrhage is excluded.
  • VTE prophylaxis per local protocol; early mobilisation and multidisciplinary rehabilitation (physiotherapy, occupational therapy, speech and language therapy).
  • Decompressive hemicraniectomy may be considered in selected patients under 60 with malignant MCA infarction and life-threatening oedema.

6.3 Secondary prevention

  • Antiplatelet/anticoagulant therapy: long-term antiplatelet (e.g. clopidogrel monotherapy) for non-cardioembolic stroke; anticoagulation for atrial fibrillation or other cardioembolic sources, usually started days after the event depending on infarct size and haemorrhagic transformation risk.
  • High-intensity statin therapy regardless of baseline LDL, per current secondary prevention guidance.
  • Blood pressure control to target once the acute phase has passed.
  • Carotid revascularisation (endarterectomy or stenting) for symptomatic significant carotid stenosis, ideally within two weeks of the event.
  • Lifestyle measures: smoking cessation, glycaemic control, weight management, alcohol moderation, and structured exercise.

7. Key trials & latest studies

Selected landmark and contemporary trials that shaped current practice. This list is illustrative for learning purposes — always check for more recent evidence and your local guidance.

NINDS rt-PA Stroke Study 1995

Established intravenous alteplase within 3 hours of onset as effective in improving functional outcome at 3 months, forming the original basis for thrombolysis in acute ischaemic stroke.

ECASS III 2008

Extended the evidence base for intravenous alteplase to the 3–4.5 hour window in selected patients, subsequently adopted into most international guidelines.

MR CLEAN, ESCAPE, SWIFT PRIME, EXTEND-IA, REVASCAT 2015

This series of trials established mechanical thrombectomy as standard of care for large-vessel occlusion within roughly 6 hours of onset, dramatically improving functional independence compared with medical therapy alone.

DAWN 2018

Showed benefit of thrombectomy 6–24 hours after last known well in patients selected using a clinical-imaging mismatch (clinical deficit severity out of proportion to infarct core size), extending the treatment window substantially.

DEFUSE-3 2018

Demonstrated benefit of thrombectomy in the 6–16 hour window using perfusion-imaging-based core/penumbra mismatch criteria, reinforcing imaging-guided rather than purely time-based patient selection.

Tenecteplase trials (e.g. EXTEND-IA TNK, AcT) 2018–2022

Growing evidence supports tenecteplase as a non-inferior, single-bolus alternative to alteplase, with several stroke networks transitioning to tenecteplase as their default thrombolytic.
