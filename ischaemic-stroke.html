Ischaemic Stroke

Algorithm-first summary: a time-critical emergency centred on rapid recognition, imaging-based selection, and reperfusion therapy.

Time-critical Reperfusion therapy Stroke unit care

1. Recognition & severity

  • FAST — Face, Arm, Speech, Time.
  • BE-FAST — adds Balance and Eyes for posterior-circulation strokes.
  • ROSIER — ED tool to separate stroke from mimics (score >0 = stroke likely).
  • NIHSS — quantify deficit severity (0–42); gates treatment decisions and tracks change.

Territory pointers for large-vessel occlusion

TerritoryTypical features
MCAContralateral face/arm > leg weakness and sensory loss; homonymous hemianopia; aphasia (dominant) or neglect (non-dominant).
ACAContralateral leg > arm weakness; abulia/personality change; gait apraxia.
PCAContralateral homonymous hemianopia (often macula-sparing); visual agnosia; may have minimal motor deficit.
VertebrobasilarVertigo, diplopia, ataxia, dysarthria, crossed sensorimotor signs, reduced consciousness; locked-in syndrome with basilar occlusion.

2. Hyperacute imaging & selection

  • Non-contrast CT head — first step to exclude haemorrhage; early ischaemic signs may be subtle (loss of grey–white differentiation, sulcal effacement, hyperdense artery sign).
  • CT angiography (head + neck) — identify large-vessel occlusion (ICA, M1/M2) amenable to thrombectomy.
  • CT perfusion / MR diffusion–perfusion — core-vs-penumbra mismatch for extended-window thrombectomy selection (DAWN, DEFUSE-3).

3. Acute reperfusion therapy

3.1 Intravenous thrombolysis

  • Alteplase 0.9 mg/kg (max 90 mg; 10% as bolus, remainder over 60 min) — or tenecteplase 0.25 mg/kg single bolus — within 4.5 hours of onset / last known well in eligible patients.
  • Perfusion-imaging selection may extend treatment to wake-up or unknown-onset stroke.

Contraindications to thrombolysis

Intracranial haemorrhage on imaging, active internal bleeding, recent major surgery or head trauma, severe uncontrolled hypertension. Always follow your local thrombolysis checklist — see Local Guidelines.

3.2 Mechanical thrombectomy

  • Confirmed large-vessel occlusion (ICA, proximal MCA M1/M2), up to 24 hours from last known well in patients selected by perfusion/collateral imaging (DAWN, DEFUSE-3).
  • Complementary to thrombolysis where both are indicated ("bridging therapy"), not a replacement.

3.3 Blood pressure around reperfusion

  • Before thrombolysis: lower to <185/110 mmHg, then maintain <180/105 mmHg for 24 hours afterwards.
  • If not thrombolysed: permissive hypertension supports penumbral perfusion — avoid aggressive lowering unless extreme.

4. Acute supportive care

  • Admit to a specialist stroke unit — reduces death and disability across all severities.
  • Maintain normoglycaemia, normothermia, and adequate oxygenation.
  • Swallow screen before oral intake to reduce aspiration risk.
  • Antiplatelet: aspirin 300 mg at 24 hours post-thrombolysis, or immediately if no thrombolysis/thrombectomy given, once haemorrhage is excluded.
  • VTE prophylaxis per local protocol; early mobilisation and MDT rehabilitation (physiotherapy, occupational therapy, speech and language therapy).
  • Decompressive hemicraniectomy: consider in selected patients under 60 with malignant MCA infarction and life-threatening oedema.

5. Secondary prevention

  • Antithrombotics: long-term antiplatelet (e.g. clopidogrel 75 mg monotherapy) for non-cardioembolic stroke; anticoagulation for AF/cardioembolic sources, timing guided by infarct size and haemorrhagic-transformation risk (usually days after onset).
  • Lipids: high-intensity statin regardless of baseline LDL.
  • Blood pressure: lower to target once the acute phase has passed.
  • Carotid revascularisation: endarterectomy or stenting for symptomatic significant carotid stenosis, ideally within 2 weeks of the event.
  • Lifestyle: smoking cessation, glycaemic control, weight management, alcohol moderation, structured exercise.

6. Advanced aetiological investigations

  • Carotid imaging (Doppler, CTA, or MRA) — for extracranial carotid stenosis amenable to revascularisation.
  • Prolonged cardiac monitoring (Holter / extended) — detect paroxysmal atrial fibrillation.
  • Echocardiography (transthoracic ± transoesophageal) — suspected cardioembolic source, cryptogenic stroke, or younger patients.
  • Antiphospholipid and targeted thrombophilia testing — younger patients or where a prothrombotic state is suspected.
  • Sickle cell workup — where sickle cell disease is a possible mechanism.
  • Catheter angiography (DSA) and vessel-wall imaging — selectively where dissection, vasculitis, or another arteriopathy is suspected.

7. Key trials & latest studies

Selected landmark and contemporary trials that shaped current practice. This list is illustrative for learning purposes — always check for more recent evidence and your local guidance.

NINDS rt-PA Stroke Study 1995

Established intravenous alteplase within 3 hours of onset as effective in improving functional outcome at 3 months, forming the original basis for thrombolysis in acute ischaemic stroke.

ECASS III 2008

Extended the evidence base for intravenous alteplase to the 3–4.5 hour window in selected patients, subsequently adopted into most international guidelines.

MR CLEAN, ESCAPE, SWIFT PRIME, EXTEND-IA, REVASCAT 2015

This series of trials established mechanical thrombectomy as standard of care for large-vessel occlusion within roughly 6 hours of onset, dramatically improving functional independence compared with medical therapy alone.

DAWN 2018

Showed benefit of thrombectomy 6–24 hours after last known well in patients selected using a clinical-imaging mismatch (clinical deficit severity out of proportion to infarct core size), extending the treatment window substantially.

DEFUSE-3 2018

Demonstrated benefit of thrombectomy in the 6–16 hour window using perfusion-imaging-based core/penumbra mismatch criteria, reinforcing imaging-guided rather than purely time-based patient selection.

Tenecteplase trials (e.g. EXTEND-IA TNK, AcT) 2018–2022

Growing evidence supports tenecteplase as a non-inferior, single-bolus alternative to alteplase, with several stroke networks transitioning to tenecteplase as their default thrombolytic.
