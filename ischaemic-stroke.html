Ischaemic Stroke

Focal neurological dysfunction caused by infarction of brain, spinal cord, or retinal tissue — the most common stroke subtype (~85%), and a time-critical medical emergency.

~85% of strokes Time-critical Reperfusion therapy

Scope of this page

This page summarises the UK National Clinical Guideline for Stroke (2023), AHA/ASA (2019/2021, updated 2026), and ESO positions, plus the major randomised trials. It is a learning aid — not a substitute for full guidelines, local protocols, the BNF, or clinical judgement. Verify all doses, time windows, and eligibility against current local and national guidance before clinical use.

1. Definition & classification

Ischaemic stroke is an episode of focal neurological dysfunction caused by infarction of brain, spinal cord, or retinal tissue. It accounts for roughly 85% of all strokes (the remainder being haemorrhagic — see Haemorrhagic Stroke).

Two useful classifications:

  • Bamford / Oxford (clinical): TACI (total anterior circulation infarct), PACI (partial anterior circulation infarct), LACI (lacunar infarct), POCI (posterior circulation infarct). Useful at the bedside for territory and prognosis.
  • TOAST (aetiological): large-artery atherosclerosis, cardioembolism, small-vessel occlusion (lacunar), stroke of other determined aetiology, and stroke of undetermined aetiology (cryptogenic). Drives secondary-prevention strategy.

2. Hyperacute assessment

  • Rapid recognition (FAST), airway/breathing/circulation, glucose.
  • Immediate non-contrast CT head to exclude haemorrhage; CT angiography to identify large-vessel occlusion (LVO); CT perfusion or MRI for extended-window selection.
  • Determine time of onset (or last-known-well), NIHSS, pre-morbid function (mRS), and treatment eligibility.

3. Intravenous thrombolysis

  • Alteplase 0.9 mg/kg within 4.5 hours of known onset remains standard in many centres (NINDS 1995; ECASS III for 3–4.5 h).
  • Tenecteplase 0.25 mg/kg is increasingly preferred as a single bolus and is supported by trials such as AcT and EXTEND-IA TNK; it is now recommended in several updated guidelines.
  • Extended / unknown-onset window: patients with wake-up stroke or unknown onset may be eligible using imaging mismatch — WAKE-UP (MRI DWI–FLAIR mismatch) and EXTEND (perfusion mismatch up to 9 h).

Screen carefully for contraindications

Recent haemorrhage, surgery, coagulopathy, uncontrolled hypertension. Thrombolysis does not replace thrombectomy where an LVO is present — give both where indicated ("drip and ship" / "mothership"). Always follow your local thrombolysis protocol — see Local Guidelines.

4. Mechanical thrombectomy (endovascular therapy, EVT)

The single biggest advance in acute stroke care. Indicated for large-vessel occlusion (typically ICA, M1, and selected M2, plus basilar). Only about 7–10% of ischaemic strokes are eligible.

4.1 Early window (0–6 hours, anterior circulation)

Standard of care for proximal anterior LVO. Evidence base: the HERMES pooled analysis (2016) of five 2015 trials — MR CLEAN, ESCAPE, EXTEND-IA, SWIFT PRIME, REVASCAT.

4.2 Extended window (6–24 hours, anterior circulation)

Selection is imaging-based, not time-based, and requires advanced imaging (CT perfusion or DWI/perfusion MRI):

  • DAWN (2018): 6–24 h, clinical–core mismatch.
  • DEFUSE-3 (2018): 6–16 h, perfusion–core mismatch.

4.3 Large ischaemic core

A wave of 2022–2024 trials showed benefit even with large established cores (low ASPECTS 3–5):

  • RESCUE-Japan LIMIT (2022), SELECT2 (2023), ANGEL-ASPECT (2023), TENSION (2023), plus TESLA and LASTE.
  • Benefit in very extensive core (ASPECTS 0–2) remains limited/uncertain.

4.4 Posterior circulation / basilar artery occlusion

Basilar occlusion is catastrophic if untreated. After earlier neutral trials (BEST, BASICS), two positive trials changed practice:

  • ATTENTION (2022): within 12 h.
  • BAOCHE (2023): 6–24 h.

Vertebrobasilar imaging (CTA/MRA) should be part of the acute LVO assessment, and posterior-circulation LVO warrants active consideration for EVT, sometimes in extended windows given the poor natural history.

4.5 Distal / medium-vessel occlusion (MeVO)

An active research area. Recent randomised trials (e.g. ESCAPE-MeVO, DISTAL, presented 2024–2025) have not shown clear benefit of EVT for medium/distal-vessel occlusions, so routine thrombectomy for MeVO is not established — decisions are individualised.

5. Malignant MCA infarction & decompressive hemicraniectomy

A large MCA-territory infarct can cause life-threatening cytotoxic oedema ("malignant" MCA syndrome), usually within 2–5 days, with very high mortality under medical management alone.

Evidence:

  • Pooled analysis of DECIMAL, DESTINY and HAMLET (patients ≤ 60, surgery within 48 h): decompressive hemicraniectomy markedly reduced mortality and increased survival with mRS ≤ 4 and ≤ 3 (numbers-needed-to-treat as low as ~2 for survival).
  • DESTINY II (patients > 60): reduced mortality, but most survivors have moderate-to-severe disability — so decisions require careful discussion of acceptable outcomes.

Typical selection features: large MCA-territory infarct (≥ 50% of territory / ≥ 2/3 on imaging), high NIHSS, declining consciousness, and ability to operate early (within ~48 h). Involve neurosurgery early and have the goals-of-care conversation with family.

Cerebellar (posterior fossa) infarction with brainstem compression or obstructive hydrocephalus is a separate neurosurgical emergency.

6. Secondary prevention

The antiplatelet, lipid, blood-pressure, and atrial-fibrillation strategies for ischaemic stroke are the same as for TIA — see the TIA page for the full detail. In brief:

  • Antiplatelets: short-course DAPT (clopidogrel + aspirin 21 days → clopidogrel) for minor stroke (NIHSS ≤ 3) or high-risk TIA; long-term clopidogrel monotherapy. Anticoagulate if AF.
  • Lipids: high-intensity statin (e.g. atorvastatin 80 mg) treat-to-target (UK non-HDL < 2.5 / LDL < 1.8 mmol/L; ESC LDL < 1.4); escalate with ezetimibe, then PCSK9 inhibitors / inclisiran / bempedoic acid.
  • Blood pressure: optimise once stable.
  • Carotid disease: symptomatic significant carotid stenosis (anterior-circulation events) should be assessed urgently for carotid endarterectomy or stenting, ideally within 2 weeks.

7. Investigating the cause (including young / cryptogenic stroke)

Standard: MRI + MRA head and neck, carotid Doppler, ECG and prolonged cardiac monitoring (for paroxysmal AF), echocardiography (TTE ± transoesophageal for PFO / endocarditis / thrombus), and vascular risk bloods.

See the TIA page for the young-stroke workup, the role and timing of catheter angiography (including posterior-circulation events), thrombophilia/antiphospholipid testing, and the sickle cell pathway.
