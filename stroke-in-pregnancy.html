Stroke in Pregnancy

Ischaemic stroke, haemorrhage, and venous thrombosis in pregnancy and the puerperium — where every imaging and treatment decision has two patients.

Peripartum risk MDT decisions Anticoagulation choices

1. Overview

Stroke risk in pregnancy is concentrated in the third trimester and the puerperium. The differential is distinctive: pre-eclampsia/eclampsia and its complications, cerebral venous sinus thrombosis, reversible cerebral vasoconstriction syndrome (RCVS) and posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES), arterial dissection, cardioembolism, and haemorrhage from eclampsia, aneurysm, or arteriovenous malformation. Urgent brain imaging should never be withheld because of pregnancy — MRI is preferred where available, and CT with abdominal shielding is acceptable when speed matters.

Treatment is individualised through urgent multidisciplinary discussion (stroke, obstetrics, neuroradiology): pregnancy is not an absolute contraindication to thrombolysis, and mechanical thrombectomy is feasible and often the preferred reperfusion strategy for large-vessel occlusion. For anticoagulation, low-molecular-weight heparin is the agent of choice — warfarin is teratogenic and DOACs are avoided in pregnancy and breastfeeding. Blood pressure control and magnesium underpin management when pre-eclampsia/eclampsia is the driver.

2. Key points

Essentials

  • Highest risk period: third trimester and the puerperium; always consider CVST, pre-eclampsia/eclampsia, RCVS/PRES, and dissection.
  • Never withhold urgent imaging because of pregnancy — MRI preferred; CT with shielding when urgent.
  • Thrombolysis is not absolutely contraindicated; thrombectomy is feasible — decide urgently with an MDT.
  • Anticoagulate with LMWH: warfarin is teratogenic, DOACs are avoided in pregnancy and breastfeeding.
  • Treat severe hypertension and use magnesium sulfate where eclampsia is the cause.
