Stroke Mimics & Chameleons

A practical guide to conditions that imitate stroke (mimics) and strokes that hide as something else (chameleons) — a learning aid, not a substitute for senior review or local hyperacute pathways.

Glucose first Don't delay treatment Check reversible causes

The overriding principle

Do not let the possibility of a mimic delay treatment of a genuine stroke — but always check the reversible causes first. Check capillary glucose immediately in any suspected stroke: hypoglycaemia is the classic reversible mimic.

1. Why this matters

A significant proportion of suspected strokes turn out to be mimics. Getting this right matters in two directions:

  • Mimics can be given thrombolysis unnecessarily. Thrombolysis in mimics is generally low-risk in published series, but it is still avoidable harm and cost — so a quick, structured assessment is worthwhile.
  • Chameleons — genuine strokes presenting atypically — are dangerous when missed, because the patient loses access to time-critical treatment.

2. Common stroke mimics

  • Hypoglycaemia (and hyperglycaemia) — can cause focal deficits; correct and reassess.
  • Seizure and Todd's paresis — post-ictal focal weakness resolving over minutes to hours; look for a witnessed seizure, tongue-biting, incontinence, post-ictal confusion.
  • Migraine with aura (including hemiplegic migraine) — aura typically spreads and evolves over minutes (positive then negative symptoms), often with headache; contrast with the sudden, maximal-at-onset deficit of stroke.
  • Functional neurological disorder — inconsistent examination, Hoover's sign, give-way weakness; a diagnosis of positive features, not just exclusion.
  • Syncope / pre-syncope — global rather than focal; rarely a true stroke mimic unless posterior circulation.
  • Sepsis / infection with recrudescence — infection or metabolic stress can unmask an old stroke deficit ("recrudescence"); the deficit is in the distribution of a prior stroke and improves as the illness is treated.
  • Bell's palsy (peripheral VII) — involves the forehead (unlike a central facial palsy, which spares the forehead).
  • Peripheral vestibular disorders (vestibular neuritis, BPPV) versus posterior-circulation stroke — use the HINTS examination in acute vestibular syndrome (a central pattern — normal head-impulse, direction-changing nystagmus, skew deviation — suggests stroke).
  • Space-occupying lesions — tumour, abscess, or chronic subdural haematoma (especially older patients, anticoagulated, or after minor head injury).
  • Demyelination (multiple sclerosis) — subacute onset, younger patients.
  • Metabolic / toxic — hyponatraemia, hepatic encephalopathy, Wernicke's encephalopathy, drug or alcohol intoxication.
  • Transient global amnesia — isolated anterograde amnesia with repetitive questioning, resolving within 24 hours.
  • Hypertensive encephalopathy / PRES — headache, visual disturbance, seizures with very high blood pressure and characteristic posterior imaging changes.

3. Stroke chameleons

Strokes that don't look like strokes:

  • Posterior circulation strokes — dizziness/vertigo, nausea, diplopia, ataxia, or isolated symptoms that get mislabelled as labyrinthitis or migraine.
  • Acute confusion / delirium — some strokes (e.g. certain right-hemisphere or thalamic strokes) present as confusion or behavioural change rather than obvious weakness.
  • Reduced consciousness — basilar occlusion and large bleeds.
  • Limb-shaking TIA — jerking movements from critical carotid stenosis, easily mistaken for focal seizures.
  • Isolated cortical signs — aphasia without weakness, neglect, or a visual field defect the patient hasn't noticed.
  • Movement disorders — hemichorea/hemiballismus from basal ganglia strokes.

4. A structured assessment approach

A pragmatic bedside sequence for the suspected acute stroke — checking reversible causes and discriminating mimics without delaying reperfusion for a genuine stroke:

flowchart TD
    A["Suspected acute stroke"] --> B["Check capillary glucose immediately"]
    B --> C{"Hypoglycaemic?"}
    C -->|Yes| D["Treat and reassess — deficit may resolve"]
    C -->|No| E["Onset and tempo: sudden and maximal at onset favours stroke; spreading/evolving favours migraine aura or seizure"]
    E --> F["Witnessed seizure, post-ictal state, tongue-biting? Consider Todd's paresis"]
    F --> G["Fever/infection with a deficit matching an OLD stroke? Consider recrudescence"]
    G --> H["Forehead involved in facial weakness? Consider peripheral (Bell's) palsy"]
    H --> I["Acute vestibular syndrome? Apply HINTS to separate central (stroke) from peripheral"]
    I --> J["Urgent CT (and CTA/perfusion as per pathway) — exclude haemorrhage and mimics like subdural or tumour"]
    J --> K{"Findings and clinical picture consistent with acute ischaemic stroke?"}
    K -->|Yes| L["Proceed on the hyperacute stroke pathway — do NOT let mimic-hunting delay treatment"]
    K -->|Uncertain / suggests mimic| M["Senior/stroke review; treat the identified cause; MRI if diagnosis remains unclear"]

5. Key teaching points

At the bedside

  • Glucose first, every time.
  • Tempo and evolution are the most useful discriminators: stroke is typically sudden and maximal at onset; migraine aura spreads over minutes; seizures have a post-ictal course.
  • Forehead sparing distinguishes central from peripheral facial weakness.
  • HINTS (performed by trained clinicians) outperforms early MRI for separating central from peripheral causes of acute vertigo.
  • When in genuine doubt about a disabling deficit within the treatment window, err towards treating the stroke after excluding haemorrhage and hypoglycaemia — missed strokes cause more harm than the small risk of treating a mimic.
  • MRI is the arbiter when the diagnosis remains uncertain after the acute phase.
