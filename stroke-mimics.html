Stroke Mimics and Chameleons

Conditions that imitate stroke, and strokes that hide as something else — with the bedside discriminators that separate them at the front door.

Differential diagnosis Bedside assessment Front-door medicine

1. Overview

Around one in four patients presenting as a suspected acute stroke has a stroke mimic — most commonly seizure with post-ictal (Todd's) paresis, migraine with aura, hypoglycaemia, functional neurological disorder, syncope, sepsis unmasking an old deficit, or a structural lesion such as a tumour or subdural haematoma. Missing a mimic risks unnecessary thrombolysis; missing a stroke risks a lost reperfusion window.

Stroke chameleons are the reverse problem: genuine strokes that present atypically and get mislabelled — posterior circulation events presenting as isolated vertigo or vomiting, thalamic or right-hemisphere strokes presenting as confusion, limb-shaking TIAs from critical carotid stenosis mimicking seizures, and cortical hand infarcts mimicking a peripheral nerve lesion. Useful bedside discriminators include the abruptness of onset, negative (loss-of-function) versus positive (spreading, evolving) symptoms, an immediate capillary glucose in every patient, and careful attention to posterior-circulation signs.

2. Key points

Essentials

  • Always check capillary glucose first — hypoglycaemia is the classic reversible mimic.
  • Sudden-onset, maximal-at-onset negative symptoms favour stroke; spreading or evolving positive symptoms favour migraine aura or seizure.
  • Common mimics: seizure/Todd's paresis, migraine aura, hypoglycaemia, functional disorder, syncope, sepsis with re-emergence of an old deficit.
  • Common chameleons: posterior circulation stroke as isolated vertigo, delirium from thalamic/right-hemisphere infarction, limb-shaking TIA.
  • When in doubt in the thrombolysis window, senior review and urgent imaging — do not let uncertainty cause unexamined delay.
