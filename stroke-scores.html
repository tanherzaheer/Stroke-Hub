Stroke Scores: ASPECTS & mRS

Two of the most frequently quoted scores in acute stroke care: ASPECTS, which quantifies early ischaemic change on the baseline CT, and the modified Rankin Scale, which grades functional outcome.

ASPECTS 0–10 mRS 0–6 Selection & outcome

How to use this page

A learning aid — not a substitute for local protocols or clinical judgement. These scores are companions to the Ischaemic Stroke and Mechanical Thrombectomy pages, where they feed directly into reperfusion decisions.

1. ASPECTS — Alberta Stroke Program Early CT Score

ASPECTS is a 10-point topographic score that quantifies early ischaemic change within the middle cerebral artery (MCA) territory on the baseline non-contrast CT. It offers a more reproducible alternative to the older "one-third of the MCA territory" rule.

ASPECTS = 10 − (number of affected regions)

The MCA territory is divided into 10 regions, assessed across two standardised axial CT levels:

  • Ganglionic level (at the basal ganglia and thalamus): caudate (C), lentiform nucleus (L), internal capsule (IC), insular ribbon (I), and cortical regions M1, M2, M3.
  • Supraganglionic level (just above the basal ganglia): cortical regions M4, M5, M6.

Start from 10 and subtract 1 point for each region showing early ischaemic change (parenchymal hypoattenuation or focal swelling). A normal CT scores 10; a score of 0 indicates diffuse ischaemia throughout the MCA territory.

Worked example

A baseline CT shows early hypoattenuation in the insular ribbon (I), the lentiform nucleus (L), and cortical region M2. Three regions are affected, so ASPECTS = 10 − 3 = 7.

Why ASPECTS matters

  • ASPECTS 6–10: generally favourable for mechanical thrombectomy in the early window.
  • ASPECTS 3–5 (large core): thrombectomy is still beneficial on the basis of the 2022–2024 large-core trials — a low ASPECTS is no longer an automatic exclusion.
  • ASPECTS 0–2: very extensive established infarct; benefit uncertain — individualise.

See the Mechanical Thrombectomy page for how ASPECTS combines with time window, occlusion site, and perfusion mismatch.

2. Modified Rankin Scale (mRS)

The modified Rankin Scale is the most widely used measure of global disability and dependence after stroke. It runs from 0 (no symptoms) to 6 (death) and is the primary functional outcome in most stroke trials. It is used both to describe pre-morbid function (a key part of thrombectomy candidacy) and to grade outcome at follow-up (commonly 90 days).

The modified Rankin Scale (mRS)
GradeDescription
0No symptoms at all.
1No significant disability despite symptoms; able to carry out all usual duties and activities.
2Slight disability; unable to carry out all previous activities, but able to look after own affairs without assistance.
3Moderate disability; requiring some help, but able to walk without assistance.
4Moderately severe disability; unable to walk and attend to own bodily needs without assistance.
5Severe disability; bedridden, incontinent, and requiring constant nursing care and attention.
6Dead.

Reading mRS in practice

  • mRS 0–2 is generally taken as "functional independence" — the usual threshold for a good outcome and for pre-morbid thrombectomy eligibility.
  • mRS 3 is the walking/dependence boundary — able to walk but needs some help.
  • A shift analysis across the whole scale (not just a single cut-point) is now the standard way trials report treatment effect.

Pre-morbid mRS is one of the four pillars of thrombectomy selection — see Mechanical Thrombectomy.
