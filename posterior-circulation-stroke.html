Posterior Circulation Stroke

A focused guide to vertebrobasilar (posterior circulation) ischaemia — the territory most often missed, and the site of some of the most lethal but treatable strokes.

Frequently missed Basilar occlusion is lethal High index of suspicion

1. Why posterior circulation is different

The posterior (vertebrobasilar) circulation supplies the brainstem, cerebellum, thalami, and occipital lobes. Posterior-circulation strokes are frequently missed or misdiagnosed as benign conditions (labyrinthitis, migraine, vestibular neuritis) because symptoms can be non-specific — yet basilar artery occlusion is one of the most lethal strokes if untreated. A high index of suspicion is essential.

2. Presentation

Think posterior circulation when you see combinations of the “5 Ds” and related signs:

  • Dizziness / vertigo (especially with other neurology)
  • Diplopia and other eye-movement abnormalities
  • Dysarthria
  • Dysphagia
  • Dystaxia (ataxia / incoordination)

Plus: visual field loss (homonymous hemianopia from occipital/PCA infarcts), crossed signs (ipsilateral cranial nerve + contralateral limb signs — a brainstem hallmark), Horner’s syndrome, nausea/vomiting, reduced consciousness (basilar), and sometimes isolated symptoms that are easy to dismiss.

3. Named brainstem/cerebellar syndromes

Illustrative patterns that localise the lesion:

4. Imaging pitfalls

  • Plain CT is insensitive in the posterior fossa (bone artefact) and often misses early brainstem/cerebellar infarcts.
  • MRI with diffusion-weighted imaging is far more sensitive, but small brainstem infarcts can still be falsely negative early — a normal early DWI does not exclude posterior-circulation stroke if the clinical picture fits.
  • CT/MR angiography of the vertebrobasilar system is essential when an occlusion or dissection is suspected — do not stop at the anterior circulation.

5. Acute vestibular syndrome — HINTS

For a patient with acute, continuous vertigo, nystagmus, and nausea (acute vestibular syndrome), the HINTS examination (performed by trained clinicians) helps separate a dangerous central cause (stroke) from a benign peripheral one:

  • Head Impulse — a normal head-impulse test is reassuring for peripheral but worrying for central in this context.
  • Nystagmus — direction-changing nystagmus suggests central.
  • Test of Skew — vertical skew deviation suggests central.

Use HINTS correctly

A “central” HINTS pattern outperforms early MRI for detecting stroke in this setting. HINTS is only valid in continuous acute vestibular syndrome with nystagmus, not in isolated positional vertigo.

6. Basilar artery occlusion and thrombectomy

Basilar occlusion has a poor natural history, which justifies an aggressive approach:

  • Intravenous thrombolysis if within window and no contraindication.
  • Mechanical thrombectomy: after earlier neutral trials (BEST, BASICS), the ATTENTION (within 12 hours) and BAOCHE (6–24 hours) trials supported thrombectomy for basilar occlusion, shifting practice toward active intervention — often in extended windows given how devastating untreated basilar occlusion is.

Time-critical and treatable

Basilar occlusion is time-critical and treatable — thrombolysis and thrombectomy (including in extended windows) save lives. Do not let non-specific early symptoms delay imaging of the vertebrobasilar vessels.

7. Key teaching points

Remember

  • Vertigo + any other neurology = posterior circulation until proven otherwise.
  • Isolated vertigo is usually peripheral, but do not anchor.
  • Plain CT reassurance is false reassurance in the posterior fossa.
  • Image the vertebrobasilar vessels — vertebral dissection is a leading cause in younger patients.
  • Basilar occlusion is time-critical and treatable — thrombolysis and thrombectomy (including extended windows) save lives.
  • Use HINTS correctly in acute vestibular syndrome.
