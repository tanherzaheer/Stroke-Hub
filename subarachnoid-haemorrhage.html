Subarachnoid Haemorrhage

Thunderclap headache until proven otherwise: diagnosis, grading, securing the aneurysm, and the complications that follow the bleed.

Thunderclap headache Neurosurgical emergency Nimodipine

1. Overview

Spontaneous subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH) is most often caused by rupture of a saccular ("berry") aneurysm. The classic presentation is a thunderclap headache — maximal within seconds to a minute — with or without collapse, vomiting, meningism, or focal deficit. Non-contrast CT performed within 6 hours of onset on a modern scanner is highly sensitive; beyond that window, or when the CT is negative but suspicion persists, lumbar puncture at ≥12 hours looking for xanthochromia remains the standard confirmatory test. CT angiography then localises the aneurysm. Clinical severity is graded with the WFNS scale, and blood load with the modified Fisher scale.

Management centres on early securing of the aneurysm — endovascular coiling is preferred over surgical clipping where the aneurysm is suitable (ISAT) — alongside oral nimodipine for 21 days to reduce delayed cerebral ischaemia, careful blood pressure and fluid management, and vigilance for the major complications: rebleeding, vasospasm/delayed cerebral ischaemia (typically days 4–14), hydrocephalus, seizures, and hyponatraemia.

2. Key points

Essentials

  • Thunderclap headache = SAH until proven otherwise; CT within 6 hours is highly sensitive, LP for xanthochromia at ≥12 hours if needed.
  • Grade clinically with WFNS; grade blood load with modified Fisher.
  • Secure the aneurysm early — coiling preferred where suitable (ISAT).
  • Nimodipine 60 mg 4-hourly for 21 days reduces delayed cerebral ischaemia.
  • Watch for rebleeding, vasospasm/DCI (days 4–14), hydrocephalus, and hyponatraemia.
