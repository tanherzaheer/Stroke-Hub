Subarachnoid Haemorrhage

Bleeding into the subarachnoid space — most often aneurysmal, and a time-critical neurosurgical emergency defined by a sudden thunderclap headache.

A neurosurgical emergency

SAH is a neurosurgical emergency — discuss every case urgently with your regional neurosciences centre. This page is an educational learning aid and is not a substitute for neurosurgical/neurocritical-care protocols or specialist judgement. Verify all doses and thresholds against current local and national guidance.

1. Definition & presentation

SAH is bleeding into the subarachnoid space. Spontaneous SAH is most often aneurysmal (~80–85%).

Classic presentation: a sudden, severe "thunderclap" headache — maximal within seconds to a minute and often described as the worst ever — frequently with neck stiffness, photophobia, nausea/vomiting, transient loss of consciousness, or a focal deficit. A sentinel ("warning") headache may precede the major bleed.

2. Causes

  • Ruptured saccular ("berry") aneurysm — the dominant cause.
  • Perimesencephalic non-aneurysmal SAH — a benign pattern with blood confined around the midbrain and a negative angiogram; better prognosis, but still needs vascular imaging to exclude an aneurysm.
  • Other causes — arteriovenous malformation, dural fistula, arterial dissection (vertebral), mycotic aneurysm, vasculitis, reversible cerebral vasoconstriction syndrome (RCVS), and rarely coagulopathy or tumour.

3. Diagnosis

  • Non-contrast CT head — first line, and highly sensitive when performed early (within 6 hours of onset). Sensitivity falls with time.
  • Lumbar puncture — if CT is negative but clinical suspicion remains, ideally at least 12 hours after onset to allow xanthochromia (spectrophotometry) to develop, distinguishing true SAH from a traumatic tap.
  • CT angiography (and often catheter DSA) — to identify and characterise the aneurysm and plan treatment.

4. Grading

  • Clinical: the World Federation of Neurosurgical Societies (WFNS) grade and the Hunt & Hess grade — based on conscious level and deficit; higher grades carry a worse prognosis.
  • Radiological: the modified Fisher scale — quantifies subarachnoid and intraventricular blood and predicts the risk of delayed cerebral ischaemia (vasospasm).

5. Acute management

The priorities are to secure the aneurysm early, prevent rebleeding, and prevent/treat delayed cerebral ischaemia.

5.1 Securing the aneurysm

Early treatment (ideally within 24–72 hours) by endovascular coiling or neurosurgical clipping, decided by a neurovascular MDT based on aneurysm location, morphology, and patient factors. ISAT showed better functional outcomes with coiling compared with clipping in patients where both were considered suitable, so coiling is often preferred when feasible — though clipping remains important for certain aneurysms (e.g. some MCA aneurysms, large haematomas).

5.2 Preventing rebleeding

Rebleed risk is highest before the aneurysm is secured. Use controlled blood pressure management, analgesia, a calm environment, and avoidance of straining. Balance rebleed risk against maintaining cerebral perfusion.

5.3 Nimodipine

Oral nimodipine 60 mg every 4 hours for 21 days reduces delayed cerebral ischaemia and improves outcome — a cornerstone of SAH care. (It acts mainly by improving outcome from DCI rather than simply preventing angiographic vasospasm.)

5.4 Supportive neurocritical care

Maintain euvolaemia, careful sodium and fluid balance, glucose and temperature control, VTE prophylaxis (mechanical initially), and treat seizures if they occur.

6. Key complications

  • Rebleeding: highest in the first hours — the main reason to secure the aneurysm urgently.
  • Delayed cerebral ischaemia (DCI) / vasospasm: typically days 4–14. Managed with nimodipine, maintaining euvolaemia and adequate blood pressure (induced hypertension once the aneurysm is secured), and, in refractory cases, endovascular therapy (intra-arterial vasodilators or angioplasty).
  • Hydrocephalus: acute obstructive or communicating — may need an external ventricular drain, later possibly a shunt.
  • Hyponatraemia: common — distinguish SIADH (treat with fluid balance) from cerebral salt wasting (needs volume and sodium replacement); avoid fluid restriction if the patient is hypovolaemic, as this risks DCI.
  • Other: seizures, cardiac stunning / neurogenic pulmonary oedema, and global cognitive effects.
