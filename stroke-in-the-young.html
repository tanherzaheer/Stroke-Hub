Stroke in the Young

Stroke under roughly 55 demands a different differential — dissection, PFO, thrombophilia, genetic and inflammatory disease — and a structured cause-finding workup.

Under ~55 Dissection & PFO Structured workup

1. Overview

Up to one in five ischaemic strokes occurs in adults under about 55, where conventional atherosclerotic risk factors explain less and the differential widens: cervical artery dissection (the leading identifiable cause, especially vertebral), cardioembolism including patent foramen ovale (PFO), antiphospholipid syndrome and other thrombophilias, genetic disease (Fabry disease, CADASIL), vasculitis, reversible cerebral vasoconstriction syndrome, sickle cell disease, recreational drug use, and pregnancy-related mechanisms. A substantial minority remain cryptogenic after full investigation.

The workup should be structured rather than scattergun: vessel imaging of both circulations, prolonged cardiac monitoring for paroxysmal AF, echocardiography with bubble study where PFO is suspected, targeted bloods (including antiphospholipid antibodies confirmed 12 weeks apart), and catheter angiography reserved for suspected vasculitis, subtle dissection, or vascular malformation. The full pathway — including PFO closure evidence (CLOSE, RESPECT, REDUCE) and posterior-circulation nuances — is mapped step-by-step on the Stroke Algorithms page.

2. Key points

Essentials

  • Cervical artery dissection is the leading identifiable cause — image both anterior and posterior circulations properly.
  • Prolonged ambulatory ECG monitoring for paroxysmal AF; echocardiography with bubble study when PFO is suspected.
  • Antiphospholipid antibodies must be confirmed 12 weeks apart before diagnosing APS.
  • PFO closure benefits selected younger patients with otherwise cryptogenic stroke (CLOSE, RESPECT, REDUCE).
  • Catheter angiography (DSA) is reserved for suspected vasculitis, subtle dissection, or vascular malformation after non-invasive imaging.
