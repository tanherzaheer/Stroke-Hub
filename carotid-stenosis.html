Carotid Stenosis

Managing extracranial internal carotid artery stenosis — including endarterectomy versus stenting and the 2025 CREST-2 results.

A rapidly evolving area

This page summarises the evidence and current debate on managing carotid stenosis, including the 2025 CREST-2 results. It is a learning aid — not a substitute for vascular/stroke MDT decisions, local protocols, or specialist judgement. Asymptomatic disease in particular is an area of active debate; check updated guidelines before applying to patient care.

1. Why it matters

Carotid atherosclerosis accounts for a meaningful proportion of ischaemic strokes. The key clinical distinction is symptomatic (a stroke, TIA, or transient monocular blindness in the territory of the stenosis within the preceding ~6 months) versus asymptomatic — because the balance of benefit from intervention is very different between the two.

Measuring stenosis: the degree of stenosis is usually reported by the NASCET method (comparing the residual lumen at the stenosis to the normal distal ICA). Duplex ultrasound, CTA and MRA are used; catheter angiography is now rarely needed purely for measurement.

2. Symptomatic carotid stenosis

2.1 Who benefits from revascularisation

Pooled data from NASCET and ECST established that carotid endarterectomy (CEA) plus medical therapy reduces recurrent stroke in symptomatic patients:

  • 70–99% stenosis: clear benefit.
  • 50–69% stenosis: moderate benefit (greater in men, older patients, and recent events).
  • Under 50% stenosis: no benefit from surgery.

Near-occlusion is a special case where the benefit is less certain.

2.2 Timing — treat early

Benefit is highly time-dependent. The risk of recurrent stroke is highest in the first days after the event, and the absolute benefit of surgery falls sharply with delay (Rothwell pooled analysis). Guidelines recommend revascularisation as soon as the patient is stable, ideally within 2 weeks of the index event. (Whether very early surgery, within 48 hours, is better or riskier is still under study.)

2.3 CEA versus carotid artery stenting (CAS)

CREST (2010) found a similar overall composite of stroke/MI/death, but with a trade-off: CEA had a lower periprocedural stroke rate, while CAS had a lower periprocedural MI rate. Peri-procedural stroke risk with stenting was higher, particularly in older patients (over 70). ICSS and long-term CREST follow-up showed similar long-term durability between the two.

Practical synthesis

CEA is generally preferred for most symptomatic patients, especially those over 70. CAS is reasonable for patients at high surgical risk, hostile neck anatomy (e.g. prior radiation/surgery), or restenosis, at experienced centres. All patients receive best medical therapy (antiplatelet + high-intensity statin + risk-factor control) regardless.

3. Asymptomatic carotid stenosis — an evolving picture

Historically, ACAS and ACST showed a small absolute stroke-reduction benefit from CEA in asymptomatic patients, but this was based on older medical therapy. As modern intensive medical therapy has driven event rates down, the value of routine intervention has been increasingly questioned (SPACE-2, ECST-2).

3.1 CREST-2 (published November 2025)

Two parallel randomised trials in patients with asymptomatic ≥70% stenosis, each comparing revascularisation plus intensive medical therapy against intensive medical therapy alone:

  • Stenting arm: adding CAS to intensive medical therapy significantly reduced the 4-year stroke/death outcome (roughly 2.8% vs 6.0%).
  • Endarterectomy arm: adding CEA did not reach statistical significance over intensive medical therapy alone (roughly 3.7% vs 5.3%).

Periprocedural risks were low across all groups, and disabling strokes were uncommon.

3.2 How to interpret it

CREST-2 is important but contested. Points raised in the subsequent commentary:

  • The medical-therapy arm achieved excellent risk-factor control (over 70% at BP and lipid targets) that may be hard to reproduce in routine practice — which could understate the real-world value of intervention.
  • Conversely, the results reinforce that intensive medical therapy alone is a legitimate strategy for many asymptomatic patients, and that blanket revascularisation is not justified.
  • Some specialty societies stated the findings do not overturn existing standards of care.

Bottom line for teaching

For asymptomatic stenosis, the decision is individualised — weighing stenosis severity/progression, life expectancy, procedural risk, patient preference, and how good the medical optimisation is. This is a genuine area of ongoing debate; keep an eye on updated guidelines.
