Carotid Stenosis

Symptomatic versus asymptomatic disease, endarterectomy versus stenting, and why timing after a symptomatic event matters more than almost anything else.

Secondary prevention CEA vs stenting CREST-2

1. Overview

Atherosclerotic stenosis of the internal carotid artery causes stroke mainly by artery-to-artery embolism. For symptomatic stenosis (ipsilateral TIA, amaurosis fugax, or non-disabling stroke), the NASCET and ECST trials established carotid endarterectomy (CEA) for 50–99% stenosis, with the greatest benefit at 70–99% — and the benefit falls steeply with delay, so revascularisation should ideally happen within two weeks of the index event. Near-occlusion and complete occlusion are managed differently.

For asymptomatic stenosis, the historical trials (ACAS, ACST) predate modern intensive medical therapy, and contemporary practice increasingly questions routine revascularisation. The CREST-2 trials (reported 2025) directly tested whether CEA or stenting adds benefit on top of modern intensive medical management in asymptomatic high-grade stenosis — their results now anchor this decision, alongside patient-specific features (stenosis progression, silent infarcts, embolic signals). Where revascularisation is chosen, CEA and stenting are compared in CREST: stenting carries more periprocedural stroke (especially in older patients), CEA more periprocedural myocardial infarction.

2. Key points

Essentials

  • Symptomatic 50–99% stenosis: revascularise early — benefit is greatest within 2 weeks of the event (NASCET/ECST).
  • All patients: intensive medical therapy — high-intensity statin, antiplatelet, blood pressure control, smoking cessation, diabetes management.
  • Asymptomatic stenosis: decision-making now rests on the 2025 CREST-2 evidence comparing intensive medical therapy alone against revascularisation plus medical therapy.
  • CEA vs stenting: stenting has higher periprocedural stroke risk (notably in older patients); CEA has higher periprocedural MI risk (CREST).
  • Carotid imaging (Doppler/CTA/MRA) is urgent after any anterior-circulation TIA or minor stroke.
