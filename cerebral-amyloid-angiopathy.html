Cerebral Amyloid Angiopathy (CAA)

A leading cause of lobar intracerebral haemorrhage in older adults — its subtypes, the imaging signs, the Boston criteria v2.0, and the wider differential of haemorrhagic stroke.

Lobar haemorrhage Small-vessel disease Boston criteria v2.0

1. What CAA is

CAA is a small-vessel disease caused by deposition of amyloid-β in the walls of cortical and leptomeningeal arteries (sparing deep perforators). It is the commonest cause of spontaneous lobar ICH in the elderly, and also causes transient focal neurological episodes (“amyloid spells”), cognitive decline, and convexity subarachnoid haemorrhage. It is distinct from hypertensive small-vessel disease, which causes deep (basal ganglia, thalamus, pons) bleeds.

2. Subtypes to recognise

2.1 Non-inflammatory CAA (the common form)

The typical presentation: recurrent lobar haemorrhages and microbleeds in an older patient, driven by amyloid deposition without a prominent inflammatory response. Managed by blood-pressure control, carefully weighing antithrombotics, and treating the consequences.

2.2 CAA-related inflammation (CAA-ri)

An inflammatory / autoimmune response to vascular amyloid — important because it is treatable. Features:

  • Subacute presentation: cognitive decline, behavioural change, seizures, headache, focal deficits.
  • Imaging: asymmetric, confluent white-matter hyperintensity (vasogenic oedema), often extending to subcortical U-fibres, on a background of multiple lobar microbleeds and/or cortical superficial siderosis.
  • Treatment: often responds to immunosuppression (corticosteroids ± further immunotherapy) — so recognising it matters.

A related entity, ABRA (amyloid-β-related angiitis), is the more florid vasculitic form.

Teaching point

A CAA phenotype (lobar microbleeds/siderosis) plus subacute encephalopathy and asymmetric white-matter oedema should prompt consideration of CAA-ri and specialist referral — it is one of the treatable causes.

2.3 Iatrogenic and hereditary CAA

Rarer forms: iatrogenic CAA (a “prion-like” transmission described decades after cadaveric dura/growth-hormone exposure or neurosurgery, presenting in younger patients) and hereditary CAA (e.g. Dutch-type APP mutations).

3. The radiological signs

On blood-sensitive MRI (T2*-GRE or SWI) and CT:

  • Lobar intracerebral haemorrhage — cortical/subcortical, often occipital/parietal; may show finger-like projections or subarachnoid extension (Edinburgh criteria features on CT).
  • Cerebral microbleeds — small round foci of signal loss on GRE/SWI, in a strictly lobar distribution (deep microbleeds point instead to hypertensive disease).
  • Cortical superficial siderosis (cSS) — curvilinear “tramline” haemosiderin staining along the cortical surface; highly suggestive of CAA.
  • Convexity (non-aneurysmal) subarachnoid haemorrhage — over the cerebral convexities, a cause of amyloid spells.
  • White-matter features — severe enlarged perivascular spaces in the centrum semiovale, and a multispot pattern of white-matter hyperintensities; posterior-predominant white-matter change.

4. Boston criteria v2.0 (2022) — simplified

Applies to patients aged 50 or over presenting with lobar ICH, cognitive impairment, transient focal neurological episodes, or convexity SAH, with no other cause and no deep haemorrhagic lesions. It combines haemorrhagic markers (lobar ICH, strictly lobar cerebral microbleeds, cortical superficial siderosis, convexity SAH) with non-haemorrhagic white-matter markers (severe centrum semiovale perivascular spaces, or a multispot white-matter hyperintensity pattern).

  • Probable CAA: two strictly lobar haemorrhagic lesions, or one strictly lobar haemorrhagic lesion plus one white-matter feature.
  • Possible CAA: one haemorrhagic lesion or one white-matter feature.

Boston v2.0 improved sensitivity over the previous v1.5 by incorporating the white-matter (non-haemorrhagic) features. Definitive diagnosis still requires pathology.

5. The wider differential — causes of haemorrhagic stroke

Use location and patient factors to structure the differential (see also the Haemorrhagic Stroke page):

The common structural / “rare but important” lobar causes

  • Arteriovenous malformation (AVM) — commonest structural cause in younger patients; artery-to-vein shunt without capillary bed.
  • Cavernous malformation (cavernoma) — “popcorn”/mulberry lesion with haemosiderin rim; recurrent small bleeds and seizures.
  • Dural arteriovenous fistula, developmental venous anomaly.
  • Aneurysm (can cause focal lobar/SAH-pattern bleeds), haemorrhagic tumour (e.g. melanoma, renal, choriocarcinoma metastases), cerebral venous sinus thrombosis, vasculitis, and RCVS.

6. Key teaching points

  • Lobar bleed in an older patient → think CAA; deep bleed → think hypertensive disease.
  • CAA-ri is the treatable subtype — recognise subacute encephalopathy with asymmetric white-matter oedema on a CAA background, and refer.
  • Use blood-sensitive MRI (SWI/GRE) to find microbleeds and cortical superficial siderosis.
  • Apply Boston criteria v2.0 for probable/possible CAA (age ≥ 50, no deep bleeds, no other cause).
  • In younger patients or atypical bleeds, hunt for a structural cause (AVM, cavernoma, aneurysm) and exclude venous thrombosis and secondary causes.
