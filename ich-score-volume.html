ICH Score & Haematoma Volume (ABC/2)

Two practical tools for intracerebral haemorrhage (ICH): the ICH Score for early prognosis, and the ABC/2 method for estimating haematoma volume.

Prognostic score Volume estimation Intracerebral haemorrhage

Critical caveat

Prognostic scores must never be used in isolation to justify early withdrawal of care, as this risks a self-fulfilling prophecy. This is a learning aid — not a substitute for local protocols or clinical judgement.

1. Estimating haematoma volume — the ABC/2 method

Haematoma volume is one of the strongest predictors of outcome in ICH and feeds directly into the ICH Score. The ABC/2 formula gives a rapid bedside estimate of the volume of an ellipsoid haematoma from the CT.

Volume (mL) = (A × B × C) / 2
  • A = the greatest diameter of the haematoma (cm) on the CT slice where it is largest.
  • B = the diameter perpendicular to A (cm) on that same slice.
  • C = the approximate vertical extent (cm) = the number of slices on which the haematoma is seen × the slice thickness (cm). A common refinement counts each slice by how much of the haematoma it contains — full slices count as 1, partial slices as 0.5.

All three measurements are in centimetres, giving a volume in millilitres.

Worked example

A haematoma measures A = 5 cm and B = 4 cm on its largest slice, and appears on 8 slices of 0.5 cm thickness, so C = 8 × 0.5 = 4 cm.
Volume = (5 × 4 × 4) / 2 = 40 mL.

2. The ICH Score

The ICH Score (Hemphill, 2001) is a simple, validated tool for 30-day mortality risk stratification, using five variables scored at presentation.

Total = 0 to 6. A higher score corresponds to higher 30-day mortality; in the original cohort, mortality rose steeply with each point (roughly 0% at a score of 0, rising to very high mortality at scores of 5–6).

Important caveat

The ICH Score is a population-level prognostic tool. Early aggressive-care limitation based on the score can create a self-fulfilling prophecy. Guidelines advise against early DNACPR/withdrawal decisions in the first 24–48 hours based on prognostic scores alone — give full active care and reassess.
