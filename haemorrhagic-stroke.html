Haemorrhagic Stroke

Algorithm-first summary of intracerebral and subarachnoid haemorrhage — blood pressure, anticoagulant reversal, and aneurysm securing are time-critical.

High early mortality Blood pressure critical Reverse anticoagulation

1. Recognition & grading

  • ICH: sudden focal deficit with headache, vomiting, reduced consciousness, and progressive worsening over minutes–hours (haematoma expansion); severe hypertension common at presentation.
  • SAH: sudden "thunderclap" headache maximal at onset ("worst headache of my life"), ± transient loss of consciousness, neck stiffness, photophobia, vomiting.

Red flags for SAH

Thunderclap headache, headache on exertion/sex/Valsalva, new headache with neck stiffness or photophobia, and headache with reduced consciousness or focal neurology — urgent CT ± lumbar puncture to exclude SAH.

  • GCS and NIHSS to document severity.
  • ICH score — predicts 30-day mortality.
  • WFNS and Hunt and Hess grades — SAH severity.

2. Diagnostic imaging & investigations

  • Non-contrast CT head — first-line and highly sensitive for acute blood; defines location, volume (ABC/2 method), intraventricular extension, and mass effect.
  • CT angiography — "spot sign" (marker of ongoing bleeding/expansion risk in ICH); identifies a causative aneurysm in SAH.
  • Lumbar puncture — if SAH is suspected but CT is normal (CT sensitivity falls after ~6 hours); classically shows xanthochromia.
  • Digital subtraction angiography (DSA) — gold standard for aneurysm characterisation; often combined with endovascular treatment.
  • Coagulation profile (INR, APTT, platelets) — identify coagulopathy to reverse.
  • MRI with susceptibility-weighted imaging — subacute phase, or to look for underlying cavernoma/microbleeds suggesting amyloid angiopathy.

Related: cerebral amyloid angiopathy & lobar bleeds

Cerebral amyloid angiopathy (CAA) is a leading cause of lobar haemorrhage in older adults and includes a treatable inflammatory subtype (CAA-ri). For the imaging signs, the Boston criteria v2.0, and the wider differential of lobar bleeding, see the Cerebral Amyloid Angiopathy page.

3. Acute management — intracerebral haemorrhage

  • Blood pressure: rapidly lower systolic to ~130–140 mmHg in the hyperacute phase (INTERACT2) to limit haematoma expansion, per local protocol.
  • Reverse anticoagulation urgently: prothrombin complex concentrate (PCC) + vitamin K for warfarin; idarucizumab for dabigatran; andexanet alfa (where available) or PCC for factor Xa inhibitors.
  • Neurosurgery: for large or cerebellar haematomas causing brainstem compression/hydrocephalus, or a surgically accessible cause. Routine evacuation of supratentorial ICH is not clearly beneficial (STICH/STICH II); cerebellar haemorrhage with deterioration is a clearer indication.
  • Raised ICP: head-up positioning, analgesia/sedation, osmotherapy if needed; correct hyperglycaemia and hyperthermia; monitor on a stroke/neurocritical care unit.
  • VTE prophylaxis: intermittent pneumatic compression initially; pharmacological prophylaxis once bleeding has stabilised, per local protocol.

Time-critical

Any patient on anticoagulation with suspected ICH needs emergency reversal as soon as haemorrhage is confirmed on CT — do not wait for further tests to delay treatment.

4. Acute management — subarachnoid haemorrhage

  • Secure the aneurysm: endovascular coiling or surgical clipping, ideally within 24–72 hours, to reduce rebleeding risk.
  • Nimodipine 60 mg PO every 4 hours for approximately 21 days to reduce delayed cerebral ischaemia from vasospasm.
  • Blood pressure: avoid hypotension (ischaemia) and extreme hypertension (rebleeding) before the aneurysm is secured.
  • Complications: hydrocephalus (may need external ventricular drain), seizures, hyponatraemia (SIADH or cerebral salt wasting), and delayed cerebral ischaemia (clinical monitoring ± transcranial Doppler, with induced hypertension and/or endovascular therapy if it occurs).

Same-day action

Suspected SAH needs same-day neurosurgical/neurointerventional discussion.

5. Key trials & latest studies

INTERACT2 2013

Intensive early blood pressure lowering (target systolic <140 mmHg) in acute ICH improved functional outcome compared with a more conservative target, supporting current intensive blood pressure protocols.

ATACH-2 2016

Compared intensive versus standard blood pressure lowering in acute ICH; did not show a clear reduction in death or disability with very intensive targets and raised caution about renal adverse effects, nuancing the INTERACT2 findings.

STICH and STICH II 2005 / 2013

Randomised trials of early surgical evacuation versus initial conservative treatment for supratentorial ICH; found no clear overall benefit from routine early surgery, though subgroups (e.g. superficial lobar haematomas) may benefit — surgical decision-making remains individualised.

ISAT (International Subarachnoid Aneurysm Trial) 2002, long-term follow-up ongoing

Compared endovascular coiling with surgical clipping for ruptured aneurysms suitable for either technique; showed better independent survival with coiling, driving the shift toward endovascular treatment as first-line where feasible.

Andexanet alfa reversal studies (e.g. ANNEXA-4) 2019 onward

Evaluated targeted reversal of factor Xa inhibitor-related intracranial haemorrhage, an increasingly relevant question given the growing use of direct oral anticoagulants.

Staying current

Minimally invasive haematoma evacuation techniques and refined blood-pressure and reversal protocols are active areas of research. Cross-check this summary against current NICE, AHA/ASA, and ESO guidance for the latest recommendations.
