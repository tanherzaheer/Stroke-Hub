Haemorrhagic Stroke

About this page

An educational resource for healthcare professionals, summarising UK, AHA/ASA and ESO guidance and the major randomised trials for intracerebral haemorrhage. A learning aid — not a substitute for full guidelines, local neurosurgical/critical-care protocols, the BNF, or clinical judgement. Verify all doses and thresholds against current local and national guidance. Aneurysmal bleeding is covered on the dedicated Subarachnoid Haemorrhage page.

1. Definitions

Haemorrhagic stroke covers bleeding into or around the brain. The two broad categories are:

  • Intracerebral haemorrhage (ICH): bleeding into the brain parenchyma (~10–15% of strokes).
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH): bleeding into the subarachnoid space, most often aneurysmal (covered on its own dedicated pathway).

This page focuses mainly on spontaneous ICH.

2. Causes and mechanisms

Identifying the underlying cause guides acute management and long-term prevention.

2.1 Hypertensive (deep) haemorrhage

The commonest cause. Chronic hypertension damages small perforating arteries (lipohyalinosis, Charcot–Bouchard microaneurysms), producing deep bleeds in the basal ganglia, thalamus, pons and cerebellum.

2.2 Cerebral amyloid angiopathy (CAA)

Amyloid-β deposition in cortical/leptomeningeal vessels causes lobar haemorrhages, typically in older patients, often recurrent. Suggested by lobar location, cortical superficial siderosis and multiple lobar microbleeds on blood-sensitive MRI (see the Boston criteria).

2.3 Vascular malformations

  • Arteriovenous malformation (AVM): abnormal direct artery-to-vein connections without a capillary bed; a common cause of ICH in younger patients. Managed by a neurosurgery / interventional neuroradiology / radiosurgery MDT (note the ARUBA trial raised questions about intervening in unruptured AVMs).
  • Cavernous malformation (cavernoma): low-flow lesions with a characteristic "popcorn"/mulberry appearance and haemosiderin rim on MRI; can cause recurrent small bleeds and seizures.
  • Dural arteriovenous fistula and developmental venous anomaly are also part of the differential.

2.4 Aneurysms

Saccular ("berry") aneurysms usually cause SAH but can cause focal ICH. Screen for aneurysm as a cause of SAH-pattern or lobar bleeds.

2.5 Other causes

  • Anticoagulant / antiplatelet-associated haemorrhage (a growing cause with DOAC/warfarin use).
  • Haemorrhagic transformation of an ischaemic infarct.
  • Coagulopathy, thrombocytopenia, liver disease.
  • Venous: cerebral venous sinus thrombosis can cause (often lobar, sometimes bilateral) haemorrhage — investigate with CT/MR venography.
  • Tumour (primary or metastatic — e.g. melanoma, renal, choriocarcinoma), vasculitis, reversible cerebral vasoconstriction syndrome (RCVS), and illicit drugs (cocaine, amphetamines).

A CT/MR angiogram (± delayed imaging) is often needed to look for an underlying structural cause, especially in younger patients, lobar bleeds, or where the pattern is atypical for hypertension.

3. Acute medical management

3.1 Blood pressure

Early intensive BP lowering to a systolic target of around 140 mmHg is commonly used (INTERACT2). ATACH-2 cautioned that aggressive lowering to below 130 did not add benefit and may increase renal adverse events, so avoid overly aggressive reduction. Follow local protocol; smooth, controlled lowering and avoiding large variability matter.

3.2 Reversal of anticoagulation / coagulopathy

  • Warfarin: stop, give IV vitamin K plus four-factor prothrombin complex concentrate (PCC) to correct the INR rapidly.
  • Dabigatran: idarucizumab.
  • Factor Xa inhibitors (apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban): andexanet alfa where available, or PCC per local protocol.
  • Correct thrombocytopenia/coagulopathy as indicated (routine platelet transfusion for antiplatelet-associated ICH is not recommended — the PATCH trial showed harm).

3.3 Supportive care

Neuro-observations, ICP awareness, glucose and temperature control, seizure treatment (not routine prophylaxis), DVT prophylaxis (mechanical initially), and early neurosurgical/critical-care involvement. Reverse and monitor; repeat imaging if deterioration (haematoma expansion is common in the first hours).

4. Surgery for intracerebral haemorrhage

Historically disappointing, but the evidence shifted in 2024. Decisions are anatomy- and patient-specific and made with neurosurgery.

4.1 Traditional craniotomy — the STICH trials

STICH (2005) and STICH II (2013): routine early open surgical evacuation of supratentorial ICH did not improve outcomes overall. STICH II hinted at a small benefit for superficial lobar haematomas without intraventricular extension.

4.2 Minimally invasive evacuation — ENRICH (2024)

ENRICH (NEJM 2024): early (within 24 h) minimally invasive trans-sulcal parafascicular surgery for lobar ICH (haematoma volume 30–80 ml) improved functional outcome and reduced 30-day mortality. The anterior basal ganglia arm was stopped for futility — so the benefit is for lobar bleeds. This marks a genuine shift toward parenchyma-sparing, minimally invasive strategies.

MISTIE III (2019): catheter aspiration + alteplase was neutral overall, but achieving good haematoma reduction (residual under 15 ml) was associated with better outcomes — technique and target volume matter.

4.3 Decompressive craniectomy — SWITCH (2024)

SWITCH (Lancet 2024): decompressive craniectomy (without clot removal) for severe deep supratentorial ICH offered a life-saving/functional signal — an option in selected severe deep bleeds with mass effect.

4.4 Cerebellar (posterior fossa) haemorrhage

A neurosurgical emergency in its own right. Surgical evacuation is generally recommended for cerebellar ICH that is large (often cited as over 3 cm diameter) or causing brainstem compression or obstructive hydrocephalus. An external ventricular drain alone is usually not sufficient when there is brainstem compression.

4.5 A note on "burr hole" surgery

Burr-hole drainage is the standard operation for chronic subdural haematoma (a collection over the brain surface, often in older patients after minor trauma), not for spontaneous intraparenchymal ICH. For ICH, the relevant options are minimally invasive evacuation (ENRICH-type), craniotomy (selected lobar), decompressive craniectomy (severe deep), and EVD/evacuation for cerebellar bleeds with hydrocephalus.

5. Secondary prevention after ICH

  • Blood pressure control is the cornerstone of preventing recurrent ICH.
  • Restarting antithrombotics after ICH is a nuanced, individualised decision balancing the indication (e.g. AF, mechanical valve) against re-bleeding risk (especially in probable CAA / lobar bleeds). Involve the MDT; timing and agent choice depend on cause and location.
  • Lipid-lowering in haemorrhagic stroke is judged on overall cardiovascular risk and the underlying cause, not applied automatically.
  • Treat the underlying lesion (AVM, cavernoma, aneurysm) per specialist MDT.
