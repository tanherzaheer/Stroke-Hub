Haemorrhagic Stroke

Bleeding into or around the brain parenchyma — less common than ischaemic stroke, but with substantially higher early mortality.

~15% of strokes High early mortality Blood pressure critical

1. Definition & classification

Haemorrhagic stroke refers to bleeding into the brain or its surrounding spaces, causing focal or global neurological injury from a combination of direct tissue damage, mass effect, and raised intracranial pressure. It accounts for roughly 10–15% of all strokes but a disproportionately high share of stroke deaths.

Two main subtypes are covered on this page:

  • Intracerebral haemorrhage (ICH): bleeding directly into the brain parenchyma, most often due to chronic hypertensive vasculopathy or cerebral amyloid angiopathy.
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH): bleeding into the subarachnoid space, most commonly due to rupture of a saccular (berry) aneurysm; a distinct and highly time-critical emergency.

Other causes of intracranial haemorrhage include arteriovenous malformations, cavernomas, anticoagulant/antiplatelet-related bleeding, haemorrhagic transformation of an ischaemic infarct, vasculitis, and, in younger patients, illicit drug use (e.g. cocaine, amphetamines).

2. Pathophysiology

Intracerebral haemorrhage

Chronic hypertension causes lipohyalinosis and microaneurysm (Charcot-Bouchard aneurysm) formation in small perforating arteries, predisposing to rupture, classically in the basal ganglia, thalamus, pons, and cerebellum. Cerebral amyloid angiopathy, caused by amyloid-β deposition in small cortical and leptomeningeal vessels, is a common cause of lobar haemorrhage in older, normotensive patients. Once bleeding starts, an expanding haematoma causes direct mechanical injury, and secondary injury follows from perihaematomal oedema, inflammation, and raised intracranial pressure. Haematoma expansion in the first hours is a major determinant of outcome.

Subarachnoid haemorrhage

Most spontaneous SAH results from rupture of a saccular aneurysm at the circle of Willis, releasing blood under arterial pressure into the subarachnoid space and causing a sudden, severe rise in intracranial pressure. Complications include rebleeding, hydrocephalus (from disrupted CSF resorption), and delayed cerebral ischaemia from vasospasm, typically peaking days 4–14 after the bleed.

4. Clinical presentation

Clinical signs alone cannot reliably distinguish haemorrhagic from ischaemic stroke — CT imaging is mandatory — but certain features raise suspicion of haemorrhage.

  • ICH: sudden focal deficit, often with headache, vomiting, reduced consciousness, and neurological worsening over minutes to hours as the haematoma expands (in contrast to the typically static deficit of ischaemic stroke). Severe hypertension is common at presentation.
  • SAH: classically a sudden, severe "thunderclap" headache — maximal at onset, often described as "the worst headache of my life" — with or without transient loss of consciousness, neck stiffness, photophobia, and vomiting. Focal deficits may occur if there is an associated intraparenchymal haematoma or from vasospasm-related ischaemia later in the course.

Red flags for SAH

Thunderclap headache, headache with exertion/sex/Valsalva, new headache with neck stiffness or photophobia, and headache with reduced consciousness or focal neurology all warrant urgent CT ± lumbar puncture to exclude SAH.

Use the Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) and NIHSS to document severity, and grading scales such as the ICH score (predicts 30-day mortality) or the World Federation of Neurosurgical Societies (WFNS) and Hunt and Hess grades for SAH severity.

5. Investigations

  • Non-contrast CT head — first-line and highly sensitive for acute haemorrhage; identifies location, volume, intraventricular extension, and mass effect. Haematoma volume can be estimated with the ABC/2 method.
  • CT angiography — look for a "spot sign" (a marker of ongoing bleeding/expansion risk in ICH) and, in SAH, to identify a causative aneurysm.
  • Lumbar puncture — indicated if SAH is strongly suspected but CT is normal (sensitivity of CT falls after ~6 hours); classically shows xanthochromia.
  • Digital subtraction angiography (DSA) — gold standard for aneurysm characterisation and often combined with endovascular treatment in SAH.
  • Coagulation profile (INR, APTT, platelet count) and full blood count — essential to identify and reverse coagulopathy.
  • MRI with susceptibility-weighted imaging — useful in the subacute phase or to look for underlying cavernoma/microbleeds suggestive of amyloid angiopathy.

6. Management

6.1 General principles (ICH)

  • Blood pressure control: rapid lowering of systolic blood pressure to a target around 130–140 mmHg (per local protocol) in the hyperacute phase, guided by trial evidence (INTERACT2), aiming to reduce haematoma expansion.
  • Reversal of anticoagulation: urgent reversal is essential — e.g. prothrombin complex concentrate and vitamin K for warfarin, specific reversal agents (idarucizumab for dabigatran; andexanet alfa where available for factor Xa inhibitors) or PCC for direct oral anticoagulants.
  • Neurosurgical input: required for large or cerebellar haematomas causing brainstem compression/hydrocephalus, or those with a surgically accessible cause. Evidence for routine surgical evacuation of supratentorial ICH is mixed (see STICH/STICH II below); cerebellar haemorrhage with deterioration is a more clear-cut surgical indication.
  • Manage raised intracranial pressure (head-up positioning, analgesia/sedation, osmotherapy if needed), correct hyperglycaemia and hyperthermia, and monitor closely on a stroke or neurocritical care unit.
  • VTE prophylaxis with intermittent pneumatic compression initially; pharmacological prophylaxis once bleeding has stabilised, per local protocol.

6.2 General principles (SAH)

  • Urgent neurosurgical/neurointerventional referral for aneurysm securing — either endovascular coiling or surgical clipping — ideally within 24–72 hours to reduce rebleeding risk.
  • Nimodipine (oral calcium channel blocker) is given for approximately 21 days to reduce the risk of delayed cerebral ischaemia from vasospasm.
  • Careful blood pressure management: avoid hypotension (which risks ischaemia) while avoiding extreme hypertension (which risks rebleeding) before the aneurysm is secured.
  • Monitor for and treat hydrocephalus (may require external ventricular drain), seizures, hyponatraemia (SIADH or cerebral salt wasting), and delayed cerebral ischaemia (clinical monitoring ± transcranial Doppler, with induced hypertension and/or endovascular therapy if it occurs).

Time-critical actions

Any patient on anticoagulation presenting with suspected ICH needs emergency reversal without waiting for confirmatory tests to delay treatment once haemorrhage is confirmed on CT. Suspected SAH needs same-day neurosurgical discussion.

7. Key trials & latest studies

INTERACT2 2013

Intensive early blood pressure lowering (target systolic <140 mmHg) in acute ICH improved functional outcome compared with a more conservative target, supporting current intensive blood pressure protocols.

ATACH-2 2016

Compared intensive versus standard blood pressure lowering in acute ICH; did not show a clear reduction in death or disability with very intensive targets and raised caution about renal adverse effects, nuancing the INTERACT2 findings.

STICH and STICH II 2005 / 2013

Randomised trials of early surgical evacuation versus initial conservative treatment for supratentorial ICH; found no clear overall benefit from routine early surgery, though subgroups (e.g. superficial lobar haematomas) may benefit — surgical decision-making remains individualised.

ISAT (International Subarachnoid Aneurysm Trial) 2002, long-term follow-up ongoing

Compared endovascular coiling with surgical clipping for ruptured aneurysms suitable for either technique; showed better independent survival with coiling, driving the shift toward endovascular treatment as first-line where feasible.

Andexanet alfa reversal studies (e.g. ANNEXA-4) 2019 onward

Evaluated targeted reversal of factor Xa inhibitor-related intracranial haemorrhage, an increasingly relevant question given the growing use of direct oral anticoagulants.

Staying current

Minimally invasive haematoma evacuation techniques and refined blood-pressure and reversal protocols are active areas of research. Cross-check this summary against current NICE, AHA/ASA, and ESO guidance for the latest recommendations.
