Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)

Algorithm-first summary: a medical emergency requiring same-day assessment and immediate antithrombotic treatment.

Medical emergency High early stroke risk Same-day assessment

1. Immediate assessment algorithm

Same-day emergency

Treat all suspected TIA as high risk. Start antiplatelet therapy immediately (once haemorrhage/mimic excluded) and arrange specialist assessment within 24 hours of symptom onset — not a routine outpatient referral.

  • Step 1 — Exclude haemorrhage and hypoglycaemia: check capillary glucose; arrange urgent brain imaging (MRI with diffusion-weighted imaging preferred; non-contrast CT if MRI unavailable/contraindicated) to exclude haemorrhage or a structural cause before starting antithrombotics.
  • Step 2 — Start antiplatelet therapy (§2).
  • Step 3 — Specialist review within 24 hours with urgent carotid and cardiac work-up (§6).
  • Step 4 — Start secondary prevention without delay (§5).

2. Antiplatelet therapy

  • Loading: aspirin 300 mg immediately.
  • Short-course DAPT (high-risk TIA): aspirin + clopidogrel for 21 days (clopidogrel 300–600 mg load then 75 mg OD; aspirin 300 mg then 75 mg OD), started within 24 hours — evidence from CHANCE and POINT.
  • Long-term: transition to antiplatelet monotherapy — usually clopidogrel 75 mg OD.

3. Anticoagulation (cardioembolic / AF)

  • For AF-related TIA, start anticoagulation once haemorrhage is excluded and imaging confirms no large infarct, per local protocol.
  • Anticoagulation replaces antiplatelet therapy for cardioembolic sources — do not combine with the short-course DAPT regimen.

4. Carotid revascularisation

  • Symptomatic carotid stenosis ≥50–70% (per criteria): carotid endarterectomy (or stenting), ideally within 2 weeks of the event — benefit falls rapidly with delay.

5. Secondary prevention

  • Lipids: high-intensity statin therapy.
  • Blood pressure: optimise once the acute phase has settled.
  • AF: anticoagulate as in §3.
  • Lifestyle: smoking cessation, glycaemic control, alcohol moderation, structured advice.
  • Driving/occupation: advise on jurisdiction-specific driving rules and safety-critical work restrictions following TIA.

6. Advanced investigations

  • Carotid imaging (Doppler ultrasound, CTA, or MRA) — urgently for anterior-circulation TIA to identify stenosis needing revascularisation.
  • Prolonged cardiac monitoring — to detect paroxysmal atrial fibrillation where the standard ECG is non-diagnostic.
  • Echocardiography (transthoracic ± transoesophageal) — if a cardioembolic source is suspected.
  • Antiphospholipid and targeted thrombophilia testing — in younger patients or where an underlying prothrombotic state is suspected.
  • Sickle cell workup — where sickle cell disease is a possible mechanism.

7. Key trials & latest studies

EXPRESS Study 2007

Demonstrated that urgent assessment and immediate initiation of treatment after TIA/minor stroke reduced the risk of recurrent stroke by roughly 80% compared with delayed assessment, providing the core evidence for "TIA as an emergency" pathways and rapid-access clinics.

SOS-TIA 2007

A French cohort study supporting a 24-hour specialist TIA clinic model, showing substantially lower-than-predicted 90-day stroke rates when patients were assessed and treated rapidly.

CHANCE 2013

Chinese trial showing that dual antiplatelet therapy (clopidogrel plus aspirin) for 21 days after minor stroke or high-risk TIA reduced recurrent stroke compared with aspirin alone, without a significant increase in major bleeding.

POINT 2018

Confirmed the benefit of short-term dual antiplatelet therapy (clopidogrel plus aspirin, started within 12 hours) after high-risk TIA/minor stroke in a Western population, with the benefit concentrated in the first three weeks — informing current international guidance on short-course DAPT.

Staying current

Antiplatelet strategies continue to evolve, including interest in ticagrelor-based regimens (e.g. THALES) and refined patient selection for dual therapy. Check current NICE/AHA/ESO guidance for the latest recommendations.
