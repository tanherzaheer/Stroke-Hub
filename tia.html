Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)

A transient episode of focal neurological dysfunction caused by brain, spinal cord, or retinal ischaemia, without acute infarction — a medical emergency and a warning sign requiring urgent assessment.

Medical emergency Assess within 24 hours Immediate secondary prevention

Scope of this page

This page summarises guidance from the UK National Clinical Guideline for Stroke (2023), AHA/ASA (2021), and ESC/ESO where relevant. It is a learning aid, not a substitute for the full guidelines, local trust protocols, the BNF, or clinical judgement. Guidelines and drug recommendations change — always verify doses and eligibility against your current local formulary and national guidance before making a clinical decision.

1. Definition

A TIA is a transient episode of neurological dysfunction caused by focal brain, spinal cord, or retinal ischaemia, without acute infarction. Modern practice favours the tissue-based definition (no infarction on imaging) over the older time-based definition (symptoms resolving within 24 hours), because many patients with brief symptoms have infarction on diffusion-weighted MRI and should be classified as stroke.

Key teaching point

A TIA is a medical emergency and a warning sign. The risk of subsequent stroke is highest in the first hours to days after the event, which is why rapid assessment and immediate secondary prevention matter.

2. Immediate assessment & risk stratification

  • Anyone with suspected TIA should be assessed by a stroke specialist and investigated urgently — within 24 hours of symptom onset (UK guideline).
  • Give aspirin 300 mg immediately (unless contraindicated) while awaiting specialist assessment, provided intracranial haemorrhage is not suspected.
  • The ABCD2 score (Age, Blood pressure, Clinical features, Duration, Diabetes) is now de-emphasised for triage in the UK guideline — it should not be used to delay specialist assessment or imaging. It still appears in some antiplatelet-eligibility criteria (see below).
  • Imaging: MRI (including diffusion-weighted and blood-sensitive sequences) is preferred to determine territory and exclude alternative diagnoses. CT is used mainly to exclude haemorrhage where MRI is unavailable.

3. Immediate antiplatelet management

3.1 Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) — the core decision

Who is eligible (UK 2023 guideline): patients within 24 hours of onset of a TIA or minor ischaemic stroke who have a low risk of bleeding, once the diagnosis is confirmed by a stroke specialist and brain imaging has excluded intracranial haemorrhage.

The commonly used eligibility thresholds derived from the CHANCE, POINT, and THALES trials are:

  • Minor ischaemic stroke: NIHSS score ≤ 3 (some regimens use ≤ 5 — see ticagrelor below)
  • High-risk TIA: ABCD2 score ≥ 4

This is the "NIHSS of 4 or less / disabling deficit" question in practice: patients with a low NIHSS (minor, non-disabling deficit) or a high-risk TIA are the group who benefit from short-course DAPT. Patients with a larger, disabling deficit, or those receiving thrombolysis/thrombectomy, are generally not candidates for early DAPT.

3.2 If DAPT is not appropriate

Where there is high bleeding risk or a contraindication: clopidogrel 300 mg loading, then 75 mg once daily as monotherapy.

3.3 Clopidogrel and CYP2C19 (pharmacogenomics)

Clopidogrel is a prodrug activated by CYP2C19. Loss-of-function alleles reduce its antiplatelet effect. The CHANCE-2 trial showed ticagrelor was superior to clopidogrel in carriers. Some UK regions are introducing CYP2C19 pharmacogenetic testing for TIA/minor stroke; where a loss-of-function variant is identified, an alternative agent (e.g. ticagrelor) may be preferred. Check whether your trust has a testing pathway.

3.4 Special situations

  • Cervical artery dissection: DAPT (aspirin + clopidogrel) may be considered for the first 21 days, then antiplatelet monotherapy until at least 3 months (anticoagulation is an alternative in some cases — follow local/specialist guidance).
  • Cardioembolic source / AF: DAPT is not the strategy — see anticoagulation below.

4. Long-term secondary prevention

4.1 Antiplatelet therapy

  • After the DAPT course, clopidogrel 75 mg once daily is the usual long-term single agent.
  • Alternatives where clopidogrel is unsuitable: aspirin 75 mg daily, or aspirin + modified-release dipyridamole (per local protocol).

4.2 Lipid management

Principle: "lower is better." Start lipid-lowering therapy early after ischaemic stroke or TIA of atherosclerotic origin.

  • First line: high-intensity statin — e.g. atorvastatin 80 mg (or maximum tolerated dose), started as soon as possible.
  • UK targets (typical): non-HDL cholesterol < 2.5 mmol/L (non-fasting), or LDL cholesterol < 1.8 mmol/L.
  • ESC/EAS 2019 targets (stricter): LDL < 1.4 mmol/L for secondary prevention; < 1.0 mmol/L for patients with a further vascular event within 2 years on maximal therapy.

Escalation ladder if target not met:

  1. Ezetimibe — added to maximally tolerated statin (adds ~15–25% LDL reduction; ezetimibe + statin reduces recurrent stroke more than statin alone).
  2. PCSK9 inhibitors (e.g. alirocumab, evolocumab) — injectable monoclonal antibodies for those not at target on statin ± ezetimibe, subject to local eligibility criteria.
  3. Inclisiran — small-interfering RNA against PCSK9, twice-yearly dosing after initial doses.
  4. Bempedoic acid — an option for statin-intolerant patients, often combined with ezetimibe.

Note on stroke subtypes

Statins are clearly beneficial in atherosclerotic (large-artery) disease. In small-vessel disease the picture is more nuanced but statins do not increase recurrent ischaemic stroke risk. In haemorrhagic stroke, lipid-lowering is assessed on overall cardiovascular risk and the underlying cause — see the haemorrhagic stroke page.

4.3 Blood pressure

BP lowering is one of the most effective secondary-prevention measures. Aim to optimise BP once the patient is stable (avoid aggressive acute lowering in the hyperacute phase). Follow local/national targets.

4.4 Atrial fibrillation and cardioembolic TIA

  • If AF (paroxysmal or persistent) is identified, anticoagulation is indicated in preference to antiplatelet therapy, once haemorrhage is excluded and timing is appropriate.
  • DOACs are generally first line over warfarin for non-valvular AF; warfarin/valvular disease per specialist guidance.
  • Prolonged cardiac monitoring may be needed to detect paroxysmal AF in otherwise cryptogenic events.

4.5 Lifestyle and driving

  • Smoking cessation, alcohol moderation, weight, diet, and physical activity all form part of secondary prevention.
  • Driving (UK): patients must not drive for a period after a TIA/stroke and must follow DVLA rules — the exact period and notification requirements depend on the licence type and recovery. Advise patients to check current DVLA guidance.

5. Investigating the cause — including young & cryptogenic TIA

Standard workup: carotid imaging (for anterior-circulation events, to identify carotid stenosis amenable to intervention), ECG and ambulatory cardiac monitoring, echocardiography where a cardiac source is suspected, and vascular risk-factor bloods (glucose/HbA1c, lipids, renal function).

Roughly 40% of strokes in young adults are cryptogenic, which prompts extended investigation. The sections below cover the "weird and wonderful" causes — but note the evidence base and testing yield are variable, so testing should be targeted, not blanket.

5.1 Thrombophilia screening — a cautious, targeted approach

Key message

Routine inherited thrombophilia screening is NOT recommended in most patients with arterial ischaemic stroke or TIA. Inherited thrombophilias are primarily associated with venous thrombosis, and their link to arterial stroke is weak and inconsistent.

  • Inherited thrombophilias — factor V Leiden, prothrombin G20210A mutation, protein C deficiency, protein S deficiency, antithrombin (III) deficiency: little robust evidence of association with arterial stroke. Testing is generally low yield and rarely changes management. May be relevant where there is a paradoxical embolism via a PFO or a strong venous thrombotic / family history.
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome (APS) — the important exception. APS is an acquired thrombophilia and a strong risk factor for arterial stroke, especially in younger patients. Test for lupus anticoagulant, anticardiolipin antibodies (IgG/IgM), and anti-β2-glycoprotein-I antibodies. A positive result must be confirmed on repeat testing ≥ 12 weeks apart (transient positivity is common). Confirmed APS changes management (often anticoagulation) and warrants specialist input.
  • When to consider targeted testing: young/cryptogenic stroke, high-oestrogen states (pregnancy, combined hormonal contraception), unexplained venous + arterial events, livedo reticularis, recurrent miscarriage, or a suggestive family history.

5.2 Sickle cell disease

  • Sickle cell disease is an important cause of stroke, particularly in children and young adults of African/Caribbean, Mediterranean, Middle-Eastern, and South-Asian heritage.
  • Consider haemoglobinopathy screening in at-risk young patients with otherwise unexplained ischaemic events.
  • Management differs (e.g. exchange transfusion, hydroxycarbamide, transcranial Doppler surveillance in known disease) — involve haematology.

5.3 Patent foramen ovale (PFO) and cryptogenic stroke

A PFO can allow paradoxical embolism. In selected younger patients with cryptogenic stroke and a PFO with high-risk features, PFO closure may reduce recurrence — decisions are made in specialist MDT settings and depend on age, PFO anatomy, and exclusion of other causes.

5.4 Other rarer causes to keep in mind

  • Cervical/intracranial arterial dissection (often young, neck pain, trauma/sport).
  • Intracranial atherosclerotic disease (ICAD) — an important cause, especially in certain ethnic groups; managed with intensive medical therapy.
  • Vasculitis / CNS inflammatory disease, Fabry disease, CADASIL and other monogenic small-vessel diseases, infective/embolic endocarditis, and illicit-drug-associated vasospasm/vasculitis.
  • Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis can mimic — investigate with CT/MR venography if suspected.

The "young stroke screen" (extended bloods and investigations for patients under ~55 with stroke/TIA) should be guided by clinical clues and local protocol, not ordered indiscriminately — many tests are low-yield without a supporting history.

6. Quick reference summary

  • TIA = transient focal ischaemia without infarction; a medical emergency and stroke warning.
  • Aspirin 300 mg now; specialist assessment within 24 hours.
  • DAPT (clopidogrel + aspirin 21 days → clopidogrel) for minor stroke (NIHSS ≤ 3) or high-risk TIA (ABCD2 ≥ 4), within 24 h, low bleeding risk, haemorrhage excluded; PPI cover.
  • High-intensity statin, treat to target; escalate statin → ezetimibe → PCSK9i / inclisiran / bempedoic acid.
  • Optimise BP; anticoagulate if AF.
  • Do not routinely screen for inherited thrombophilia; do consider antiphospholipid syndrome and sickle cell in young/cryptogenic cases, with confirmatory repeat testing for APS.
