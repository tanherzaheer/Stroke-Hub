Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)

A transient episode of focal neurological dysfunction caused by brain ischaemia, without evidence of acute infarction — a critical warning sign requiring urgent assessment.

Medical emergency High early stroke risk Same-day assessment

1. Definition & mimics

A TIA is classically defined as a transient episode of neurological dysfunction caused by focal brain, spinal cord, or retinal ischaemia, without acute infarction on imaging (the modern tissue-based definition, replacing the older, purely time-based "symptoms resolving within 24 hours" definition). In practice, most true TIAs resolve within an hour, and any persisting deficit — or infarction found on MRI diffusion-weighted imaging even after full clinical recovery — reclassifies the event as a stroke.

TIA and minor stroke are best thought of as points on the same spectrum as ischaemic stroke, sharing the same underlying mechanisms (large-artery atherosclerosis, cardioembolism, small-vessel disease) — which is why TIA is a medical emergency, not a reassuring event.

Common stroke/TIA mimics

  • Focal (Todd's) paresis following a seizure
  • Migraine with aura
  • Hypoglycaemia
  • Peripheral vestibular causes of vertigo (e.g. BPPV)
  • Functional neurological disorder
  • Syncope (typically causes global, not focal, symptoms)
  • Bell's palsy (lower motor neurone facial weakness, spares the forehead)

2. Pathophysiology

The mechanisms causing TIA mirror those of ischaemic stroke: transient occlusion or hypoperfusion of a cerebral or retinal artery, typically from artery-to-artery embolism (e.g. from carotid atherosclerotic plaque), cardioembolism (e.g. atrial fibrillation), or small-vessel disease, with spontaneous early recanalisation or sufficient collateral flow preventing established infarction. Because the underlying vascular lesion (e.g. unstable carotid plaque, untreated atrial fibrillation) remains, the risk of a subsequent, potentially disabling stroke is highest in the first hours to days after a TIA.

3. Clinical presentation

Symptoms reflect the vascular territory involved and are, by definition, focal and transient. Common presentations include:

  • Unilateral limb and/or facial weakness or numbness
  • Dysphasia or dysarthria
  • Amaurosis fugax — transient monocular vision loss, often described as a curtain descending, due to retinal artery ischaemia (a TIA equivalent of the eye)
  • Homonymous visual field loss
  • Ataxia, vertigo, or diplopia (posterior circulation)

Symptoms that argue against TIA

Isolated dizziness, isolated memory disturbance, confusion alone, isolated double vision without other neurology, sensory symptoms confined to part of one limb, and loss of consciousness are less typical of TIA and should prompt consideration of alternative diagnoses — though posterior circulation events can present atypically, so clinical context matters.

4. Risk stratification & investigations

The ABCD2 score was historically used to stratify early stroke risk after TIA, but current guidance (e.g. NICE) increasingly recommends that all suspected TIA patients be treated as high risk and assessed urgently regardless of score, since ABCD2 alone can under-triage clinically significant cases.

FactorPoints
Age ≥ 60 years1
Blood pressure ≥ 140/90 mmHg at assessment1
Clinical features: unilateral weakness (2) or speech disturbance without weakness (1)1–2
Duration: ≥ 60 min (2) or 10–59 min (1)1–2
Diabetes mellitus1

Urgent investigations should not be delayed while awaiting specialist review:

  • Urgent brain imaging — MRI with diffusion-weighted imaging is preferred where available (more sensitive for acute ischaemia); non-contrast CT if MRI is unavailable or contraindicated, or to exclude haemorrhage/an alternative structural cause.
  • Carotid imaging (Doppler ultrasound, CTA, or MRA) — urgently for anterior-circulation TIA, since symptomatic carotid stenosis carries a high early recurrence risk and may need urgent revascularisation.
  • ECG and, where indicated, prolonged cardiac monitoring to detect paroxysmal atrial fibrillation.
  • Blood glucose, full blood count, coagulation, renal function, and fasting lipid profile.
  • Echocardiography if a cardioembolic source is suspected.

5. Management

Urgency of assessment

Per NICE guidance, patients with suspected TIA should be started on antiplatelet therapy immediately (once haemorrhage/mimics are excluded clinically) and assessed by a specialist within 24 hours of symptom onset — this is a same-day emergency, not a routine outpatient referral.

  • Immediate antiplatelet therapy: aspirin 300 mg immediately, followed by a short course of dual antiplatelet therapy (e.g. aspirin plus clopidogrel) for 21 days in selected patients per current evidence (see CHANCE/POINT below), then transition to long-term antiplatelet monotherapy — usually clopidogrel.
  • Anticoagulation: for TIA related to atrial fibrillation, anticoagulation is started once haemorrhage has been excluded and imaging confirms no large infarct, per local protocol.
  • Carotid endarterectomy or stenting: for symptomatic carotid stenosis ≥50–70% (per relevant criteria), ideally performed within two weeks of the event, as benefit diminishes rapidly with delay.
  • Vascular risk factor management: high-intensity statin therapy, blood pressure optimisation, glycaemic control, smoking cessation, and structured lifestyle advice — the same secondary prevention principles as for ischaemic stroke.
  • Driving and occupational advice: patients should be informed of relevant driving regulations (which vary by jurisdiction and license type) and safety-critical occupational restrictions following TIA.

6. Key trials & latest studies

EXPRESS Study 2007

Demonstrated that urgent assessment and immediate initiation of treatment after TIA/minor stroke reduced the risk of recurrent stroke by roughly 80% compared with delayed assessment, providing the core evidence for "TIA as an emergency" pathways and rapid-access clinics.

SOS-TIA 2007

A French cohort study supporting a 24-hour specialist TIA clinic model, showing substantially lower-than-predicted 90-day stroke rates when patients were assessed and treated rapidly.

CHANCE 2013

Chinese trial showing that dual antiplatelet therapy (clopidogrel plus aspirin) for 21 days after minor stroke or high-risk TIA reduced recurrent stroke compared with aspirin alone, without a significant increase in major bleeding.

POINT 2018

Confirmed the benefit of short-term dual antiplatelet therapy (clopidogrel plus aspirin, started within 12 hours) after high-risk TIA/minor stroke in a Western population, with the benefit concentrated in the first three weeks — informing current international guidance on short-course DAPT.

Staying current

Risk-stratification and antiplatelet strategies continue to evolve, including interest in ticagrelor-based regimens (e.g. THALES) and refined patient selection for dual therapy. Check current NICE/AHA/ESO guidance for the latest recommendations.
